It was 4:23 p.m. on October 10, 2024. Two contract workers at the PEMEX Deer Park refinery in Texas unbolted a flange — the bolted joint where two sections of pipe meet. They meant to open a different one, about five feet away, on a line that had been shut off and emptied. But the flange they actually opened was on the live Amine Unit. That line was full of pressurised acid gas — roughly 90 percent hydrogen sulfide. Over 27,000 pounds of H₂S poured out into the unit. One worker died where he stood. A second contractor, working downwind in the next unit over, also died from breathing it in. Thirteen people were taken to hospitals. The surrounding community was told to shelter in place. The gas kept escaping for almost an hour before responders could get back in and put the flange back together.

This wasn't some freak engineering failure. The equipment did exactly what it was built to do. What broke down was the chain of identification and briefing — the steps that are supposed to stop a crew from ever opening the wrong piece of pipe on a running unit.

On February 23, 2026, the U.S. Chemical Safety and Hazard Investigation Board put out its final report on the incident. If you open flanges for a living, it's a short read, and it's worth your whole lunch hour.

## What the CSB Report Actually Says

The CSB found that "the opening of incorrect equipment caused the release of pressurized hydrogen sulfide," and that PEMEX Deer Park "did not establish an effective method to clearly identify the correct equipment to open before authorizing to do so." Those are dry words for a loud finding. In plain terms: nobody made sure the right pipe was the right pipe before giving the go-ahead.

CSB Chairperson Steve Owens said it more bluntly: *"Two people died and the surrounding community was put at risk because of a completely preventable mistake."* Investigator Tyler Nelson added the line every contractor crew lead should tape to their clipboard: *"Opening hazardous process piping is a common maintenance activity that can be performed safely with effective equipment identification."*

One finding reads like a direct hit on the way most refineries lean on written procedures: *"PEMEX Deer Park had written procedures that standardize pipe marking for pipe cutting but did not have a standardized process for flange opening and blind removal activities."* Read that again. The site had a written procedure for the rarer job — cutting pipe — but not for the routine one, opening flanges. The routine one is what killed people.

The report also includes a quietly devastating "what if": *"Had PEMEX Deer Park required clear standardized markings for all line opening activities, this incident may have been prevented."*

## What Looked Routine Went Sideways

Here's how the day actually unfolded, pieced together from the report. A maintenance contractor crew started the shift in a unit that was in positive isolation — that's the gold-standard "dead" state. The line is valved out, drained, and staged for turnaround work, so there's no way for pressure or product to reach it. Positive isolation is the state every BA (breathing apparatus) specialist and millwright knows in their hands: you walk up to a flange, undo the bolts, expect some vapor and gunk but no pressure, and trust that the system behind you is dead.

Mid-shift, the crew got reassigned. The report calls it an "abrupt change" — moved to a partly running unit nearby, the Amine Regeneration Unit, which handles an acid gas stream that's nine parts H₂S to one part CO₂. The reassignment was written into a broad work permit that covered several jobs with different hazards. There were no clear hold points on that permit — no built-in moments forcing the crew to stop and re-check when the danger level changed. The permit did carry a written instruction: stop work and get an operator on the spot before opening the H₂S piping. In the field, that instruction got missed.

Picture what that reassignment feels like from the sharp end. You've spent all morning in shutdown mode. Muscle memory says isolated, drained, safe to unbolt. Someone points you at a new flange, in a unit that looks pretty much the same. The tag that names the correct flange is out of your line of sight. A parallel line carrying live, pressurised acid gas runs just five feet away. The drawings and the flange list on site aren't detailed enough to tell the two segments apart at a glance. Nobody has specifically briefed your crew on the dangers of this new unit — the CSB says so directly. And the permit doesn't force you to stop and double-check.

So you pick the wrong flange. The report says about five feet separated the correct flange from the one the crew opened. And that gap sat in an environment that did nothing to help a sensible person get it right: no positive equipment identification, an ID tag out of view, a flange list that wasn't good enough, and a permit that didn't require an operator to physically be there when the joint was broken.

H₂S at the levels released here isn't a "smell it and run" hazard. Reports cite concentrations of at least 500 ppm in the affected area. At that level, your nose stops working within seconds — that classic "rotten eggs" warning is gone before your brain even catches up. One breath of the plume at that concentration can shut your breathing down. And distance downwind matters: the second person who died was a contractor for a different company, working in the next unit over, nowhere near the leak itself.

## What the Training Card Doesn't Cover

SCC/VCA training, H₂S Alive, and every contractor onboarding deck drill the same things: positive isolation, gas testing, escape routes, what the gas does to your body. Our crews practice it. Every refinery teaches it. The PEMEX Deer Park report never claims the contractors were untrained. It says they were put in an environment where training alone couldn't save them — because the one thing that mattered most, knowing which flange was which, wasn't guaranteed by the system around them.

Here are three things the training cards don't cover well, and that this report shines a light on:

**1. The mental switch from shutdown mode to live mode is invisible from inside the crew.** When a crew moves from positive-isolation work onto a partly running unit next door, the hazard around them resets — but their state of mind doesn't. The permit system is supposed to force that reset with explicit hold points. A permit that bundles mixed-hazard jobs together without clear hold points is itself the hazard.

**2. Positive equipment identification is the whole ballgame.** Not labelling. Not a tag tied to a valve handle. Positive identification means a method that makes the person about to break containment confirm — using something in the field that can't be confused — that this exact flange is the one that's dead. The CSB has recommended that PEMEX Deer Park label all piping in the unit to the ANSI/ASME A13.1 pipe-marking standard. The Board has gone further and asked ASME to write guidelines setting a standard practice for marking equipment before opening it — with clear identifiers, and rules for taking the markings off once the work is done. That recommendation is a quiet admission that the industry has no common standard for the most common maintenance job it does.

**3. "Operator presence at the break" is not the same as "operator approved the permit."** The permit told the crew to get an operator physically at the flange before opening it. In practice, the crew opened it without that operator there. A permit instruction that relies on the crew remembering to pause, walk across the unit, and go fetch an operator — right after an abrupt reassignment, mid-shift — is a single-point-of-failure control. One thing slips, and there's nothing behind it. The right way is for the operator to be at the flange as a condition of breaking the line, not as a nice-to-have beforehand.

## The Lesson For Crew Leads and Young Techs

If you run a crew, three practical things come out of this report that you can put in place Monday morning, without waiting for some corporate programme.

First, refuse bundled permits for mixed-hazard work. If one permit covers both positive-isolation work and work on lines that are only drained-and-tagged on a running unit, ask the issuer to split it. At the very least, get explicit hold points written in — ones that force a fresh briefing and an operator at the flange at each handoff. A broad permit is cheap for the permit office and expensive for the crew.

Second, treat positive identification as a two-person check at the flange, not something that ended when the permit was issued. Walk the line with the operator. Read the tag out loud. Match it to the drawing. Confirm the break point before a single bolt comes loose. If a tag is out of view, or a drawing isn't specific enough to tell similar lines apart, stop and escalate. Those five feet between the right flange and the wrong one are the distance between a routine shift and a funeral.

Third, when your crew gets reassigned mid-shift, force the reset yourself. Pull your people aside for ninety seconds. Say out loud what's changed: the unit, the hazard class, the isolation status, the operator you're going to walk the new flange with. The CSB found that the Deer Park crew "were not specifically informed of the risks in the operational unit." That briefing isn't a gift the operator hands you. As the crew lead, it's your job to demand it.

And for young technicians heading to a first turnaround: here's the honest truth about this work. The procedures are good. The training is generally good. The incidents that kill people happen in the seams between systems — a permit that crosses a hazard boundary, a tag that's out of view, a fast handover that nobody stops to process. The experienced crew leads you'll work under are watching for those seams. Learn what they pause for.
