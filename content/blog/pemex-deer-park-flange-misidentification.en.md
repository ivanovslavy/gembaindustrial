At 4:23 p.m. on October 10, 2024, two contract workers at the PEMEX Deer Park refinery in Texas unbolted a flange. They had intended to open a different one, about five feet away, on a line that had been isolated and drained. The flange they actually opened was on the live Amine Unit — pressurised acid gas, roughly 90 percent hydrogen sulfide. Over 27,000 pounds of H₂S vented into the unit. One worker died where he stood. A second contractor employee, working downwind in an adjacent unit, also died from inhalation. Thirteen people were transported to hospitals. A shelter-in-place order went out to the surrounding community. The release ran for almost an hour before emergency responders got back in and reassembled the flange.

This was not a freak engineering failure. The equipment did what it was designed to do. What failed was the chain of identification and briefing that is supposed to keep crews from opening the wrong piece of pipe on a running unit.

On February 23, 2026, the U.S. Chemical Safety and Hazard Investigation Board released its final report on the incident. If your crew opens flanges for a living, it is a short read that is worth your whole lunch hour.

## What the CSB Report Actually Says

The CSB determined that "the opening of incorrect equipment caused the release of pressurized hydrogen sulfide" and that PEMEX Deer Park "did not establish an effective method to clearly identify the correct equipment to open before authorizing to do so." Those are dry words for a loud finding.

CSB Chairperson Steve Owens put it more plainly: *"Two people died and the surrounding community was put at risk because of a completely preventable mistake."* Investigator Tyler Nelson added the sentence that every contractor crew lead should have taped to their clipboard: *"Opening hazardous process piping is a common maintenance activity that can be performed safely with effective equipment identification."*

One specific finding reads like a direct indictment of the written-procedure culture most refineries rely on: *"PEMEX Deer Park had written procedures that standardize pipe marking for pipe cutting but did not have a standardized process for flange opening and blind removal activities."* In other words, the site had a procedure for the rarer job, but not for the routine one. The routine one killed people.

The report includes a quietly devastating counter-factual: *"Had PEMEX Deer Park required clear standardized markings for all line opening activities, this incident may have been prevented."*

## What Looked Routine Went Sideways

Here is the human shape of the day, reconstructed from the report. A maintenance contractor crew had started the shift working in a unit that was in positive isolation — valved out, drained, staged for turnaround work. Positive isolation is the state every BA specialist and millwright knows in their hands: you can step up to a flange, undo the bolts, expect vapor and residue but not pressure, and trust that the system behind you is dead.

Mid-shift, the crew was reassigned. The report describes an "abrupt change" to a partially operational unit nearby — the Amine Regeneration Unit, which processes an acid gas stream that is nine parts H₂S to one part CO₂. The reassignment was documented in a broad work permit covering multiple jobs with varying hazards. There were no clear hold points on the permit requiring the crew to pause and re-orient when the hazard profile changed. The permit also carried a written instruction to stop work and get an operator's presence before opening the H₂S piping. That instruction was overlooked in the field.

Think about what that reassignment feels like from the sharp end. You have spent the morning in shutdown mode. Muscle memory says isolated, drained, safe to unbolt. Someone points at a new flange in a unit that looks structurally similar. The tag identifying the correct flange is out of your line of sight. A parallel line carrying live, pressurised acid gas runs five feet away. The drawings and flange list on site are not detailed enough to distinguish the two segments at a glance. Your crew has not been specifically briefed on the operational hazards of the new unit — the CSB notes this explicitly. The permit does not force you to stop and validate.

You pick the wrong flange. The report says roughly five feet separated the correct flange from the one the crew opened. That gap was covered by an environment that did not help a reasonable person get it right — no positive equipment identification, an identification tag out of view, an insufficient flange list, and a permit that did not require an operator to be physically present at the break.

H₂S at the concentrations released in this incident is not a "smell and run" hazard. Reports reference concentrations of at least 500 ppm in the affected area. At that level, olfactory fatigue sets in within seconds; the classic "rotten eggs" warning is gone before a person processes what has happened. One breath of the plume at that concentration can cause respiratory paralysis. Downwind distances matter: the second fatality was a contractor for a different company working in an adjacent unit, not at the leak.

## What the Training Card Doesn't Cover

SCC/VCA training, H₂S Alive, and every contractor onboarding deck teach the same things: positive isolation, gas testing, escape procedures, the physiological effects of the gas. Our crews drill it. Every refinery teaches it. The PEMEX Deer Park report does not claim the contractors were untrained. It claims they were asked to operate in an environment where training alone could not save them, because the basic precondition — knowing which flange was which — was not guaranteed by the system around them.

Three things the training cards do not cover well, and that this report illuminates:

**1. The cognitive shift between shutdown mode and live mode is invisible from inside the crew.** When a crew moves between positive-isolation work and work on a partially operational neighbour, the hazard environment resets but the crew's frame of mind does not. The permit system is supposed to force that reset with explicit hold points. A permit that bundles mixed-hazard work without clear hold points is the hazard.

**2. Positive equipment identification is the whole ballgame.** Not labelling. Not a tag tied to a valve handle. Positive identification: a method that requires the person about to break containment to confirm, with something in the field that cannot be confused, that this exact flange is the one that is dead. The CSB has recommended that PEMEX Deer Park label all piping in the unit to the ANSI/ASME A13.1 pipe-marking standard. The Board has further asked ASME to develop written guidelines establishing a standard practice for marking equipment before opening, with clear identifiers and rules for removing the markings after work is complete. That recommendation is a quiet admission that the industry lacks a common standard for the most common maintenance activity it performs.

**3. "Operator presence at the break" is not the same as "operator approved the permit."** The permit told the crew to get an operator physically at the flange before opening. In practice, the crew opened the flange without that presence. A permit instruction that depends on the crew remembering to pause, walk across a unit, and fetch an operator — after an abrupt reassignment, in the middle of a shift — is a single-point-of-failure control. The standard procedure is for the operator to be at the flange as a condition of the line break, not as a courtesy before it.

## The Lesson For Crew Leads and Young Techs

If you run a crew, three practical things come out of this report that you can adopt Monday morning without waiting for a corporate programme.

First, refuse bundled permits for mixed-hazard work. If a single permit covers both positive-isolation work and work on lines that are only drained-and-tagged on an operational unit, ask the issuer to split it, or at minimum to write explicit hold points that require a fresh briefing and operator presence at each transition. A broad permit is cheap for the permit office and expensive for the crew.

Second, treat positive identification as a two-person verification at the flange, not a task that ended when the permit was issued. Walk the line with the operator. Read the tag aloud. Match it to the drawing. Confirm the break point before a single bolt comes loose. If a tag is out of view or a drawing is not specific enough to distinguish similar lines, stop and escalate. The five feet between the right flange and the wrong one is the distance between a routine shift and a fatality.

Third, when your crew gets reassigned mid-shift, force the reset yourself. Pull your people aside for ninety seconds. State out loud what has changed: the unit, the hazard class, the isolation status, the operator you are going to walk the new flange with. The CSB found that the Deer Park crew "were not specifically informed of the risks in the operational unit." That briefing is not the operator's gift to you. It is your job as a crew lead to demand it.

For young technicians heading to a first turnaround: the honest reality of this work is that the procedures are good, the training is generally good, and the incidents that kill people happen in the seams between systems — a permit that crosses a hazard boundary, a tag that is out of view, an abrupt handover that no one pauses to process. The experienced crew leads you will work under are watching for those seams. Learn what they pause for.
