At 12:21 in the morning on 19 November 2023, a field operator was standing next to a set of burner controls on a fired heater at Marathon Martinez Renewables, in Martinez, California. The unit was brand new and being started up for the first time. The heater was running hot — too hot. The control room could have shut it down from a screen. Instead, a person was sent out to turn the burners off by hand.

While he stood there, one of the tubes inside the heater split open. Renewable diesel and hydrogen sprayed out, found a flame, and the whole heater went up. He took third-degree burns over more than 80% of his body. He was in critical condition for over six months before he was well enough to move to a rehab facility.

The U.S. Chemical Safety Board — the CSB, the federal agency that investigates big chemical accidents the way the NTSB investigates plane crashes — published its final report on this, *Fired Heater Tube Rupture and Fire at Marathon Martinez Renewables Facility*, on 12 March 2025. The board's chairperson put the core of it in one sentence: "A series of safety failures contributed to the severity of this incident, including the lack of appropriate guidance for when to shut down the heater remotely instead of putting a worker at risk of harm."

That last phrase is the whole post. *Instead of putting a worker at risk of harm.* Here is the longer reading, for the crews who work start-ups.

## What a Fired Heater Actually Is

Quick plain-words version, because the rest only makes sense if this part does.

A **fired heater** (also called a furnace or process heater) is a big steel box with burners at the bottom and a maze of tubes running through it. The stuff you want to heat — in this case renewable diesel feed mixed with hydrogen — flows *inside* the tubes. The burners heat the *outside* of the tubes. The metal wall of the tube is the only thing keeping a flammable, high-pressure mix apart from an open flame. As long as fluid keeps moving through the tubes, that flowing fluid carries the heat away and the metal stays at a safe temperature. Stop the flow, and the empty tube has nothing to pull the heat away — so the metal climbs fast toward the point where it goes soft and lets go.

**Hydroprocessing** is the refinery step that uses hydrogen, heat and a catalyst to clean and convert feed — here, turning fats and oils into **renewable diesel**, a drop-in diesel made from biological feedstock instead of crude. Martinez was a crude-oil refinery that had been converted to make renewable fuels. New process, new unit, first start-up. Hold that thought.

## What Actually Happened

The CSB's report lays out a chain, and no single link in it is exotic. That's what makes it worth reading.

During the start-up, a manual valve upstream of the heater was left **open** when it should have been **closed**. With that valve open, most of the feed took a shortcut and bypassed the heater tubes. So the tubes that were supposed to have fluid racing through them carrying off heat instead had very little flow. That's the "low flow through fired heater" issue in the report's own list.

At the same time, the air getting to the burners was restricted. When burners don't get enough air at the burner itself, the fuel doesn't all burn where it's supposed to — it finishes burning further up, in the **convection section** (the upper part of the heater, where tubes are meant to soak up leftover heat from the exhaust gas on its way out). Fuel burning in the wrong place is called **afterburning**, and it dumps heat exactly where the equipment isn't built to take it.

Put those two together: tubes with almost no cooling flow inside them, and extra fire raging around the outside of them. The convection tubes overheated far past what they were designed for, bulged, and weakened. This is "short-term overheating" — not slow corrosion over years, but metal cooked past its limit in a matter of time you can measure on a shift.

Over 200,000 pounds of renewable diesel and around 2,200 pounds of hydrogen were released when the tube finally ruptured. The fire caused roughly **$350 million** in damage and the unit sat shut down for about a year, restarting in November 2024.

## The Decision at the Sharp End

Here is the part that should stop a crew lead cold.

By the time the heater was clearly in trouble, somebody had to act. There were two ways to kill the burners. One was to do it remotely, from the safety of the control room, with no human anywhere near the heater. The other was to send a field operator out to the unit to switch the burners off by hand — standing right next to a heater that was already overheating, with tubes already weakening.

They sent the person.

He was at the burner controls when a tube let go. The CSB's chairperson named this directly: the failure wasn't just the valve or the air or the flow. It was "the lack of appropriate guidance for when to shut down the heater remotely instead of putting a worker at risk of harm." There was no clear rule that said: *when the heater is in this condition, nobody goes near it — you trip it from the board.* So in the moment, under start-up pressure, a human body became the tool of last resort.

"Worker proximity to fired heater" is one of the six safety issues in the report. Written down like that, it sounds like a line on an audit form. Read it again knowing a man spent six months in critical care, and it reads differently. The question isn't only *what went wrong with the equipment*. It's *who did we decide should be standing next to it when it went wrong, and why didn't we have a rule that kept them away.*

## Why This Lands Hardest on a Start-Up

![A dense bank of industrial process piping, valves and lines running together — the maze where one valve left in the wrong position quietly sends flow where it shouldn't go.](/images/blog/marathon-martinez-fired-heater-tube-rupture-csb/mid.jpg)

*Image: Christian Harb on Unsplash.*

Start-up is the most dangerous window in the life of a unit, and a *first-ever* start-up of a converted unit doubles down on it. The steady-state operating manual — the one that tells you what normal looks like — barely exists yet. People are reading conditions they've never personally seen on this equipment. Procedures written on paper meet a real plant for the first time, and the gaps show up live.

Two of the CSB's six issues are really about that gap. **"Safe operating limits"** means: clear, written boundaries that say *this far and no further* — a tube metal temperature you do not cross, a minimum flow you do not drop below — with an agreed action when you hit them. **"Burner operation"** and **"valve misalignment"** are the kinds of things a mature operating procedure catches with a checklist and a second set of eyes. On a first start-up, those guardrails are often thinnest exactly when the plant is least predictable.

For contractors this matters because start-up and commissioning is when a lot of outside crews are on site — vendor reps, specialty technicians, extra operators. You may be the person closest to a piece of equipment that nobody has run before, taking direction from a control room that's also seeing this for the first time. The thing that protects you in that moment is not your own nerve. It's whether somebody, before the job, wrote down the limits and the *don't-go-near-it* rule — and whether the person giving you the radio call respects it when the pressure is on.

This is exactly the scenario SCC/VCA-trained crews drill: a deviation appears, and the discipline is to stop and act on the limit, not to push through it because the schedule wants the unit on-line. The Martinez report is a reminder that the discipline has to live in the written start-up plan, not just in the heads of the people on shift.

## The Lesson for Crew Leads and Young Techs

You can't redesign a client's heater. But you can carry a few questions onto any start-up or turnaround that has fired heaters in scope, and they're built straight from this report:

1. **Ask whether the heater can be tripped remotely — and when the rule says to use it.** The clearest finding here is that there was no good guidance on when to shut down from the board instead of sending someone out. Before the job, find out: in what condition does this heater get killed remotely, with nobody near it? If the answer is "we'd send an operator," push back.

2. **Know the safe operating limits before you start, in numbers.** Not "watch the temperature" — the actual tube metal temperature and the minimum flow, and the agreed action when you reach them. If those numbers aren't on the day's pack, that's the gap, not a detail.

3. **Treat low flow through a fired heater as an emergency, not a nuisance.** Flow is what keeps the tube metal alive. A bypass valve left open, a meter reading that doesn't add up downstream — on a heater, that's the start of the chain that cooked these tubes. Chase it immediately.

4. **Walk the line on valve positions during start-up.** A single manual valve in the wrong position diverted the flow here. Physically confirming every valve is where the procedure says — not assuming — is the cheap step that breaks this chain at the very first link.

5. **Refuse the "just send someone" reflex.** If a piece of equipment is already in an abnormal, deteriorating state, the question to ask out loud is: *does a person actually need to be next to it, or can we do this from a safe distance?* The CSB framed the whole severity of this incident around that one choice. You're allowed to ask it.

For a young tech weighing this work: nothing about that night was a freak event. A valve, some air, some flow, and a decision made under pressure about who stands where. Every one of those was catchable. The crews who come home are the ones who treat the boring questions — *which valve, what limit, who's near the heater* — as the real job, not the paperwork around it.
