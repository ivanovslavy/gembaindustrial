On 5 February 2026, Axens announced it had closed the purchase of 100% of Eurecat. If you read refinery margin reports for a living, you already filed this under "catalyst supply-chain news" and moved on. If you load and dump reactors for a living, you should read it again — because Eurecat is not a catalyst maker. Eurecat is the company that *handles* the catalyst, and one of the things it handles is the reactor your crew climbs into.

Here is the line Axens put at the top of the release: "With the closing of this acquisition, Axens reaffirms its long-term commitment to catalyst circularity and sustainable catalyst management," said Quentin Debuisschert, the company's Chairman and CEO. It is a clean corporate sentence. Nothing in it tells you that "catalyst management" is, at the sharp end, two people in breathing apparatus inside a nitrogen-filled vessel, breaking a coked bed by hand. That part never makes the press release. So here is the longer reading — what the deal is, and what "circularity" looks like at the manway.

## What Axens Actually Bought

First, plain words. A catalyst is the material packed inside a reactor that makes the chemistry happen — in a refinery, it's what turns heavy, sour feed into clean diesel under high pressure and hydrogen. It wears out over a run of one to four years, and then it has to come out and fresh material has to go in. That swap is the catalyst changeout, and it's the heart of most refinery turnarounds.

Eurecat sits across the whole life of that material. Per Axens' own description, the company does four things:

- **Regeneration, rejuvenation and recycling of spent catalyst** — taking the worn-out material, burning off the coke and restoring activity so it can run again, or recovering the metals if it can't.
- **Ex-situ activation of fresh catalyst** — getting brand-new catalyst chemically ready *outside* the reactor, before it's ever loaded, instead of doing that step inside the unit at start-up.
- **Reactor services — catalyst loading and unloading** — the physical job of getting the material into and out of the vessel.

"Ex situ" just means "outside the reactor" (in situ means "in place," inside the unit). That distinction is the whole game here, and it's where the contractor angle lives.

Eurecat runs industrial sites in the Americas, Europe, the Middle East and Asia, serving refining, biofuels, chemicals and gas. It was historically a joint venture; Axens is now the sole owner, with separate relationship and collaboration agreements signed with Ketjen on the way out the door. The strategic word in every version of the announcement is *circularity* — keep the catalyst in service longer, regenerate instead of landfill, recover the metals at the end. All good. But notice what that strategy quietly does: it pushes more and more of the catalyst's working life through Eurecat's hands, including the two moments when a crew is most exposed.

## Why a Changeout Crew Should Read a Catalyst-Services Deal

The mistake is to read this as a finance story — one big company bought another, margins, synergies, move on. For anyone who works inside reactors, it's an operations story, because it shapes two decisions a refiner makes before your crew ever arrives: **how the fresh catalyst shows up**, and **how the spent catalyst leaves**. Both decisions change what the atmosphere inside the vessel is when the head comes off.

The convenient framing of ex-situ activation is "load-and-go." The supplier activates the catalyst in their own facility, ships it ready to run, and the refiner skips the slow, fiddly in-situ activation step at start-up. Less downtime, faster to on-stream, fewer chemicals on site. That's the sales pitch, and it's largely true.

What the pitch leaves out is the handling. There's a real split here that the changeout crew inherits. An *oxidised* fresh catalyst — the metals still in oxide form — can be loaded in plain air. But a catalyst that already has the sulfur on it, which is exactly what ex-situ activation produces, is a different animal. As the industry's own technical Q&A on naphtha hydrotreaters puts it, loading catalyst "with sulfur already on it should be done in an inert environment." So the convenient, time-saving, fully-activated catalyst can be the one that turns your loading shift into an inert-entry job — nitrogen in the vessel, breathing apparatus on the loaders — rather than a straightforward air load.

It doesn't stop at the load, either. A presulfided catalyst starts reacting the moment the unit warms up — activation kicks in around 250°F and runs up past 600°F, with most of the heat coming off between roughly 400 and 550°F. That heat has to go somewhere. Many hydrotreaters don't even have thermocouples down in the bed to watch it, and dense-loaded beds — packed tight to squeeze in more catalyst — make the heat release harder to read, not easier. None of that is the loading crew's job to control. But it's the reason the loading scheme, the drop height, and the bed-level checks on the day-of-job pack are not optional paperwork. The convenience was bought somewhere upstream; the heat shows up at start-up.

## The Two Inert Atmospheres

Here's the part that ties the whole deal back to a breathing-apparatus crew. Eurecat's model — and therefore, increasingly, Axens' model — leans on moving chemistry out of the reactor and into a controlled facility. That's genuinely better engineering. Ex-situ regeneration controls temperature far better than the old in-situ burns, which were prone to runaway hot spots and gas channeling, and it recovers far more of the catalyst's activity — on the order of 75–95%, versus 50–60% for in-situ. Nobody's arguing with the chemistry.

But follow the material. If the fresh catalyst comes in pre-activated, your **load** may need an inert atmosphere. And if the spent catalyst is going *out* for regeneration rather than to landfill — which is the entire circularity premise — then it has to be **unloaded** in a state fit for transport and reprocessing, and that unload is the classic inert-entry job. Two ends of the same reactor, two nitrogen atmospheres, and the company now selling both ends of that life cycle is the same company.

## The Asphyxiation Problem Nobody Puts in a Press Release

Spent hydroprocessing catalyst is pyrophoric. That means it catches fire on its own when it meets air — the iron sulfide and coke that build up over a run will ignite spontaneously the moment oxygen reaches them, and the fire throws off sulfur dioxide, which is toxic. You cannot just open the vessel and shovel it out.

The standard answer, for decades, has been to remove the catalyst under nitrogen. Flood the reactor with an inert gas so there's no oxygen for the catalyst to react with, and the material comes out cold and safe. The catalyst is now safe. The crew is not. As the industry's own write-ups state plainly, "confined space entry into a nitrogen filled atmosphere is inherently a dangerous operation," and nitrogen "poses an extreme risk of asphyxiation to personnel." You cannot smell it, you cannot feel it, and a few breaths of pure nitrogen drop a person before they understand anything is wrong. The same sources note that there have been "numerous fatalities by asphyxiation during inert-entry activities in the refining industry even when the work is being carried out by specialist inert-entry contractors."

Read that last clause twice. *Even when the work is being carried out by specialist inert-entry contractors.* This is not a hazard you train away. It's a hazard you respect every single entry, on a rotation of primary entrant and standby, with a continuous nitrogen sweep, oxygen monitoring at the entrant's lapel, and an airline that does not kink. Crews who do this for a living — the four breathing-apparatus specialists we keep on the GEMBA core team, scaled up when a turnaround needs it — drill exactly this scenario in the SCC/VCA refresher. And the industry's own record still says people die doing it. The "circularity" sentence in the press release is built on top of this job. It just doesn't mention it.

## Passivation: The Quiet Alternative

There is a way to take the nitrogen out of the equation, and it's worth knowing because it changes the staffing math. Catalyst passivation is a technology that coats the pyrophoric catalyst and the internal surfaces with a thin organic film, which slows oxygen from reaching the reactive material. Treated this way, the spent catalyst can be unloaded **under air** — no nitrogen, no inert entry, no asphyxiation risk for the unload.

If you only weigh the safety of the people inside, passivation wins outright: it removes the single most lethal hazard of the changeout. It's not free — it's a chemical treatment with its own cost, schedule, and downstream implications for how the regenerator receives the catalyst — and that's exactly the kind of trade-off a full-service catalyst company is positioned to package. Which path a refiner picks — inert entry or passivated air unload — is no longer just a contractor's call. Increasingly it's bundled into the catalyst-services contract, decided in a procurement meeting months before anyone rigs an unloader nozzle.

## What Changes for Turnaround Planning and Staffing

Strip out the corporate language and the practical shift is this: **the company that makes the catalyst now also wants to load it, unload it, and regenerate it.** Axens-with-Eurecat can sell a refiner the catalyst, the ex-situ activation, the reactor loading, the unloading, and the regeneration of the spent material — the whole loop, one contract.

For a refiner, that's tidy. For the independent changeout and inert-entry crews who've traditionally owned the reactor-services step, it's a signal worth tracking. It doesn't mean the work disappears — someone still has to put hands and breathing apparatus inside the vessel, and integrated suppliers frequently subcontract exactly that. But it does mean the *decision* about how your crew works — inert or passivated, who supplies the standby, whose procedure governs the entry — is migrating up into a bundled services deal. The crew lead at the manway has less room to shape it and more reason to read the contract before the turnaround, not during it.

A few things worth verifying on any catalyst-changeout job on the 2026–2027 calendar, especially where an integrated catalyst-services package is in scope:

1. **Find out, early, whether the fresh charge is oxidised or pre-activated.** That single fact decides whether your load is an air job or an inert-entry job — and it changes the crew size, the BA bottle plan, and the permit.
2. **Confirm the unload method in writing: inert entry or passivated air unload.** These are completely different jobs with different hazards. Don't let it be assumed. Get it on the day-of-job pack.
3. **Pin down whose procedure and whose standby governs the entry.** In a bundled services contract, the answer is not automatically your company's. Know it before the head opens.
4. **Treat nitrogen as the lethal hazard it is, regardless of who holds the contract.** The record is clear that specialist crews still die in inert atmospheres. Rotation, continuous oxygen monitoring, a dedicated standby, and a tested rescue plan are non-negotiable on every entry.
5. **Watch the start-up heat plan on a pre-activated charge.** If the catalyst came in fully sulfided, the activation exotherm is real, the bed may be blind to thermocouples, and dense loading makes it harder to read. The loading scheme on the pack is what keeps that manageable.

For a young technician weighing reactor work as a career, here's the bigger picture in one line: the forces deciding the catalyst inside the vessel — margin, circularity, supplier consolidation — also decide the atmosphere you'll breathe inside it. A deal announced in a February press release, with not one word about confined space, quietly shapes whether your next entry is into clean air or into nitrogen. The crew lead's job is to know which one before the manway opens.
