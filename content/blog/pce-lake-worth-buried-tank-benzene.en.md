In July 2025, a tank-cleaning crew opened a subterranean gasoline storage tank fifteen feet below grade at a worksite in Lake Worth, Florida. An entrant went in. Something went wrong inside. The entrant did not come out alive. On 3 March 2026, OSHA's Atlanta regional office [issued release 26-108-ATL](https://www.osha.gov/news/newsreleases/atlanta/20260303), citing PCE Petroleum Contractors Enterprises Inc. of Land O' Lakes, Florida with twelve serious violations and a proposed penalty of $60,242. The chemicals involved were benzene and toluene — the standard aromatics that linger in any gasoline tank after the product is drained.

The citation reads like the contents page of a permit-required confined-space program: no written entry program, no atmospheric evaluation, no permit, no written respiratory protection program, no hazard communication program. Twelve serious findings. One buried tank. One worker.

This is a post about why a citation reading like that is unusual and routine at the same time — and what a contracting crew lead can do this week to make sure no version of it lands on their crew.

## What OSHA actually cited

The Atlanta news release is short. It names the company, the location, the chemicals, the count of violations, and the headline categories. The twelve serious citations, in the categories OSHA enumerated, cluster around four standards a tank-cleaning crew should already know by heart:

- **29 CFR 1910.146** — Permit-Required Confined Spaces. OSHA cited the failure to develop and implement a written PRCS program, failure to ensure authorized entrants knew the hazards and the signs and symptoms of exposure, allowing entry without atmospheric evaluation, and allowing entry without the required permit.
- **29 CFR 1910.134** — Respiratory Protection. OSHA cited the failure to maintain a written respiratory protection program for workers required to wear full-face respirators.
- **29 CFR 1910.1200** — Hazard Communication. OSHA cited the failure to implement a HazCom program where employees were exposed to chemicals.
- **29 CFR 1910.1028** — the benzene standard, with its specific permissible exposure limit of 1 ppm over an 8-hour TWA and a short-term limit of 5 ppm, sits behind the HazCom and respiratory findings.

This is not a citation about a single failed component. It is a citation about a program that did not exist in writing. The standard procedure for a tank-services contractor — a single binder that lists the entry program, the respiratory program, the HazCom program, with the trained roles, the entry permits, the gas-test data, and the SDS — was not produced on inspection.

OSHA's release paraphrases the finding cleanly: *the agency cited the employer for allowing employees to enter a confined space without an atmospheric evaluation or a required permit.*

The company has [contested the citation](https://www.insurancejournal.com/news/southeast/2026/03/06/860852.htm) before the Occupational Safety and Health Review Commission. The penalty amount and citation count may change. The facts of the entry will not.

## Why a "drained" gasoline tank still kills you

The detail that gets lost in the headline is the chemistry. A subterranean gasoline tank is not empty when the product is pumped out. It is a steel cavity, fifteen feet under grade, with three things inside that the training card has to handle separately.

First, there is residual liquid in the bottom — water bottoms, sludge, and a film of gasoline that did not lift with the suction. Second, there is vapour. Benzene boils at 80 °C and has a vapour pressure that fills a closed tank within minutes after it has been emptied. Toluene is heavier and lingers longer in the lower volume. Third, there is the geometry: a buried tank has a single manway at the top, no cross-ventilation, and a fifteen-foot vertical drop. Anything that goes wrong below has the entrant breathing through it on the climb back up.

The benzene exposure limit at 1 ppm is not a theoretical number. A gasoline-tank atmosphere immediately after de-fuelling will read in the hundreds of ppm of total VOC. Even after several air-changes, the bottom sludge continues to off-gas benzene at concentrations that overwhelm an air-purifying respirator. The standard procedure is forced ventilation, continuous atmospheric monitoring, and supplied-air breathing apparatus — not a half-mask cartridge.

Without atmospheric evaluation, none of those decisions get made on data. They get made on assumption. The assumption is that because the tank looks empty, it reads empty. The atmosphere does not work that way.

## What the training card does cover

Anyone who has been through a confined-space refresher in the last ten years can recite the steps. Identify the space. Classify it — permit-required or non-permit. Pre-entry atmospheric test for oxygen, flammables, and toxics. Forced ventilation if needed. Permit issued and posted at the manway. Standby attendant outside. Retrieval rig and harness. Continuous monitoring. Communication every fixed interval. Stop-work authority for any deviation.

Those steps fit on a laminated card. SCC/VCA-certified crews drill them every two years. Workers can answer the multiple-choice on the certification test. The card works as a script when the program around it exists. When the program does not exist, the card describes nothing the crew is required to do, because there is no permit to fail to issue, no atmospheric data to test against a limit, no written respirator schedule to enforce.

The Lake Worth citation is what that gap looks like. The training was not the missing piece. The program the training would have lived inside was.

## What the training card doesn't cover

This is where the post lives. Four things sit between the laminated card and the buried tank, and the OSHA citation traces each of them to a documentary failure rather than a knowledge failure.

**The written program is the load-bearing document, not the permit.** A permit-required confined-space program under 1910.146(c) is not the permit. It is the document that names the spaces on site, classifies them, sets the testing protocols, defines the roles (entry supervisor, entrant, attendant, rescue), specifies the equipment, and lays out the rescue plan. The permit is the per-entry instance. If the program does not exist in writing, no permit issued for a one-off job can stand in for it. OSHA's first cited violation against PCE was the absence of the written program. Everything else followed from that.

**Atmospheric evaluation is not the four-gas check.** A four-gas meter reads oxygen, LEL, CO, and H2S. A gasoline tank reads zero on H2S, near-zero on CO, possibly within range on oxygen if it has been ventilated, and may read below 10% LEL after a short purge — and still contain hundreds of ppm of benzene and toluene that the meter does not see. The atmospheric evaluation that 1910.146(d)(5) requires is *for the contaminants the entrant may be exposed to*, which means a PID for total VOC at minimum and benzene-specific monitoring for any tank that has held gasoline within the working memory of the site. A four-gas meter saying "all clear" on a gasoline tank is the wrong instrument answering a question it cannot answer.

**The respirator program is two documents, not one piece of equipment.** A full-face air-purifying respirator with organic-vapour cartridges is the wrong selection for a gasoline tank entry — the chemistry exceeds the cartridge capacity within minutes. The right selection is supplied-air, either SCBA or airline with escape bottle. But the equipment selection only happens correctly when the written respiratory program under 1910.134(c) names the contaminants, sets the assigned protection factors, schedules the medical clearances, fit-tests on cassette, and tracks the maintenance. The OSHA citation against PCE for "no written respiratory program for full-face respirator users" is the legal name for that whole missing scaffolding.

**Hazard communication is the SDS at the manway, not in the trailer.** The 1910.1200 violation is the one that reads almost ceremonial — every contractor has a HazCom binder somewhere — but the substantive failure is whether the entrant knew benzene's signs and symptoms before going down. Benzene depresses the central nervous system at exposure levels well below the immediately-dangerous-to-life-and-health threshold. The entrant's first warning is dizziness or euphoria — symptoms easily mistaken for "I'm fine, just a bit tired." The training cards generally cover this. The HazCom program is what puts the SDS in the entrant's hand on the day, with the symptoms read out at the pre-job briefing rather than skimmed off a wall-poster in March.

Four standards. Four documentary scaffolds. The training card sits on top of all four and assumes they exist.

## What a crew lead can do this week

Three things to take into the next pre-job briefing. None of them require new equipment or a software system.

**Ask to see the written PRCS program, not the permit.** If the only document anyone can produce for the tank entry is a one-page entry permit filled out the day of the job, that is the problem. The program — the binder that names the spaces, the roles, the equipment, the rescue plan — should be on the desk in the site office, with the entry supervisor named in writing. The permit is an instance of the program. Without the program, the permit is a piece of paper.

**Bring the right meter.** For any tank that has held gasoline, jet fuel, condensate, or any aromatic-bearing hydrocarbon, the pre-entry atmospheric test needs a PID at minimum. A four-gas alone is not an evaluation. Continuous monitoring inside the space during entry should be on a single device the entrant wears, with audible alarms set against the standard's PELs for benzene specifically — 1 ppm 8-hour TWA, 5 ppm short-term, 0.5 ppm action level. If the crew lead does not own a PID, the operator should. If neither does, the entry does not happen.

**Decide the respirator selection on data, not habit.** A full-face APR is the wrong choice for a gasoline tank entry regardless of how clean the pre-entry test reads, because the bottom sludge will off-gas under the heat of a worker's body and the load on the cartridge will climb during the work. The standard selection is supplied-air. The crew lead's job is to refuse APR for any gasoline-tank work and write that refusal into the job pack at the planning stage. The pushback on cost happens once, at planning. The pushback during entry happens after someone is already at the bottom of a buried tank.

The Lake Worth case will move through the Review Commission. The penalties may shift. The buried-tank geometry, the gasoline atmosphere, the benzene exposure, and the missing program will not.
