In July 2025, a tank-cleaning crew opened an underground gasoline storage tank fifteen feet below the surface at a worksite in Lake Worth, Florida. One worker climbed in. Something went wrong down there. He never came back out alive. On 3 March 2026, OSHA's Atlanta regional office [issued release 26-108-ATL](https://www.osha.gov/news/newsreleases/atlanta/20260303), citing PCE Petroleum Contractors Enterprises Inc. of Land O' Lakes, Florida with twelve serious violations and a proposed penalty of $60,242. The chemicals in play were benzene and toluene — the usual leftovers that hang around in any gasoline tank after the fuel is pumped out.

Read the citation and it's basically a checklist of everything a confined-space safety program is supposed to have — except every box is unchecked. No written entry program. No air testing. No permit. No written respirator plan. No hazard-communication plan. Twelve serious findings. One buried tank. One worker.

This is a post about why a citation like that is both strange and completely ordinary at the same time — and what a crew lead can do this week to make sure nothing like it ever lands on their team.

## What OSHA actually cited

The Atlanta news release is short. It names the company, the location, the chemicals, the number of violations, and the headline categories. Those twelve serious citations, grouped the way OSHA listed them, cluster around four rules that any tank-cleaning crew should already know cold:

- **29 CFR 1910.146** — Permit-Required Confined Spaces. This is the rulebook for entering tight, enclosed spaces you can't just walk in and out of. OSHA cited the company for never writing one of these programs down, for not making sure the workers going in knew the dangers and the warning signs of exposure, for letting someone enter without testing the air first, and for letting them enter without the required permit.
- **29 CFR 1910.134** — Respiratory Protection. This is the rule on breathing gear. OSHA cited the company for not keeping a written respirator plan for workers who had to wear full-face masks.
- **29 CFR 1910.1200** — Hazard Communication. This is the "tell your workers what chemicals they're dealing with" rule. OSHA cited the company for not running one of these programs even though employees were exposed to chemicals.
- **29 CFR 1910.1028** — the benzene rule, which sets a hard limit of 1 ppm averaged over an 8-hour day (that's "ppm," parts per million — one drop of benzene in a million drops of air), plus a short-term ceiling of 5 ppm. It sits behind both the HazCom and respirator findings.

So this isn't a citation about one broken part. It's a citation about a program that didn't exist on paper at all. The thing a tank contractor is supposed to have — one binder spelling out the entry plan, the respirator plan, the HazCom plan, with the trained roles, the permits, the air-test readings, and the chemical safety sheets — simply wasn't there when the inspector asked for it.

OSHA's release sums the finding up plainly: *the agency cited the employer for allowing employees to enter a confined space without an atmospheric evaluation or a required permit.* In plain words, they let someone go in without testing the air and without the paperwork that says it's safe to go in.

The company has [contested the citation](https://www.insurancejournal.com/news/southeast/2026/03/06/860852.htm) before the Occupational Safety and Health Review Commission, the board that hears these appeals. The penalty and the violation count might change. What happened during the entry won't.

## Why a "drained" gasoline tank still kills you

The part that gets lost in the headline is the chemistry. An underground gasoline tank isn't empty just because the fuel got pumped out. It's a steel cavity, fifteen feet underground, with three things still inside — and each one needs handling in its own way.

First, there's liquid left in the bottom — water, sludge, and a film of gasoline that the pump couldn't lift. Second, there's vapour. Benzene boils at 80 °C, which is low enough that it turns to gas and fills a closed tank within minutes of being emptied. Toluene is heavier, so it settles and hangs around even longer down low. Third, there's the shape of the thing: a buried tank has one hatch at the top, no way for fresh air to blow through, and a fifteen-foot drop straight down. So if anything goes wrong at the bottom, the worker has to breathe through it the whole way back up.

That benzene limit of 1 ppm isn't some made-up number. The air inside a gasoline tank right after the fuel's drained will read in the hundreds of ppm of total fumes. And even after you've swapped the air out a few times, the sludge at the bottom keeps releasing benzene at levels that overwhelm a basic filter mask. The right approach is to blow fresh air in, monitor the air constantly, and use breathing gear that feeds in clean air from outside — not a little half-mask with a cartridge.

Skip the air testing, though, and none of those calls get made on facts. They get made on a guess. The guess is that because the tank looks empty, the air must be clean. Air doesn't work that way.

## What the training card does cover

Anyone who's sat through a confined-space refresher in the last ten years can rattle off the steps. Spot the space. Sort it — does it need a permit or not? Test the air before going in for oxygen, for anything flammable, for anything toxic. Blow fresh air in if you need to. Issue a permit and post it at the hatch. Station a buddy outside to keep watch. Rig up a harness and a way to haul the worker out. Keep monitoring the air. Check in on a set schedule. And give anyone the power to stop the job the moment something looks off.

All of that fits on a laminated card. Certified crews drill it every two years. Workers can ace the multiple-choice on the test. The card works great as a script — as long as the program behind it actually exists. When it doesn't, the card tells the crew to do nothing, because there's no permit to fail to issue, no air reading to check against a limit, no written respirator schedule to follow.

The Lake Worth citation is what that gap looks like in real life. The training wasn't the missing piece. The program that the training was supposed to live inside — that was missing.

## What the training card doesn't cover

This is where the post really lives. Four things sit in the gap between that laminated card and the buried tank, and OSHA's citation ties each one back to a paperwork failure — not a knowledge failure.

**The written program is the document everything rests on — not the permit.** A permit-required confined-space program under 1910.146(c) isn't the permit itself. It's the document that lists every confined space on the site, sorts them, sets the air-testing rules, spells out who does what (the supervisor, the worker going in, the watcher outside, the rescue team), names the gear, and lays out the rescue plan. The permit is just the slip for one specific entry. If the program doesn't exist on paper, no permit scribbled out for a one-off job can take its place. OSHA's very first violation against PCE was the missing written program. Everything else flowed from that.

**Air testing isn't the same as the four-gas check.** A four-gas meter is the common handheld tool that reads four things: oxygen, the flammable level (LEL — basically how close the air is to catching fire), carbon monoxide, and hydrogen sulphide (H2S). Here's the catch with a gasoline tank: it'll read zero on H2S, almost nothing on carbon monoxide, maybe fine on oxygen if it's been aired out, and maybe even safe on the flammable level after a quick purge — and still be full of hundreds of ppm of benzene and toluene that the meter just can't see. The air test that 1910.146(d)(5) actually requires is *for the contaminants the worker might breathe in* — which means, at the very least, a PID (a meter that catches total fumes) plus benzene-specific monitoring for any tank that's held gasoline within living memory of the site. A four-gas meter saying "all clear" on a gasoline tank is the wrong tool answering a question it was never built to answer.

**The respirator program is two pieces of paper, not one piece of gear.** A full-face filter mask with organic-vapour cartridges is the wrong pick for a gasoline tank, because the fumes blow past what the cartridge can soak up within minutes. The right pick is supplied air — gear that feeds in clean air from a tank or an air line, with a small escape bottle as backup. But you only pick the right gear when the written respirator program under 1910.134(c) names the chemicals, sets how much protection each mask gives, schedules the medical check-ups, runs the fit-tests, and tracks the upkeep. OSHA's citation against PCE for "no written respiratory program for full-face respirator users" is the legal name for all of that missing scaffolding.

**Hazard communication means the safety sheet at the hatch, not in the trailer.** The 1910.1200 violation is the one that sounds almost like box-ticking — every contractor has a HazCom binder somewhere — but the real failure is whether the worker knew benzene's warning signs before climbing down. Benzene slows down your nervous system well below the level that's officially "immediately life-threatening." The first warning a worker gets is dizziness or a giddy, light feeling — easy to brush off as "I'm fine, just a little tired." The training cards usually cover this. The HazCom program is what actually puts the safety sheet in the worker's hand on the day, with the symptoms read out loud at the pre-job briefing — not skimmed off a wall poster back in March.

Four rules. Four paper scaffolds. The training card sits on top of all four and just assumes they're there.

## What a crew lead can do this week

Three things to carry into your next pre-job briefing. None of them need new gear or new software.

**Ask to see the written PRCS program — not the permit.** If the only thing anyone can hand you for the tank entry is a one-page permit filled out the morning of the job, that's the red flag right there. The program — the binder that lists the spaces, the roles, the gear, the rescue plan — should be sitting on the desk in the site office, with the entry supervisor named on paper. The permit is just one instance of the program. Without the program behind it, the permit's just a piece of paper.

**Bring the right meter.** For any tank that's held gasoline, jet fuel, condensate, or any other aromatic hydrocarbon, the pre-entry air test needs a PID at the very least. A four-gas alone isn't a real evaluation. And the air should be monitored non-stop while the worker's inside, on a single device they wear, with alarms set against benzene's specific limits — 1 ppm averaged over 8 hours, 5 ppm short-term, and a 0.5 ppm action level (the point where you have to start taking precautions). If the crew lead doesn't own a PID, the operator should. If neither one does, the entry doesn't happen.

**Pick the respirator on data, not habit.** A full-face filter mask is the wrong call for a gasoline tank no matter how clean the pre-entry test reads, because the sludge at the bottom keeps releasing fumes once a worker's body heat warms it up, and the load on the cartridge only climbs as the job goes on. The right call is supplied air. The crew lead's job is to flat-out refuse filter masks for any gasoline-tank work and write that refusal into the job pack at the planning stage. You take the cost pushback once, up front, at planning. The other kind of pushback comes after someone's already at the bottom of a buried tank.

The Lake Worth case will work its way through the Review Commission. The penalties may shift. The buried-tank geometry, the gasoline air, the benzene exposure, and the missing program won't.
