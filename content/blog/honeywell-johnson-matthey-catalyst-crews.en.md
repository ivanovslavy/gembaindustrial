On February 23, 2026, Honeywell and Johnson Matthey announced an amended agreement. Honeywell will acquire Johnson Matthey's Catalyst Technologies business for £1.325 billion — down from the £1.8 billion the two parties shook on back in May 2025. The long stop date (the deadline by which the deal has to close) moved from February 21, 2026 to July 21, 2026, with a possible extension to August 21, 2026 if regulatory conditions are met. Both sides expect the deal to close by the end of August 2026.

Now, are you a turnaround manager, a procurement lead, or a contractor crew chief who routinely handles KATALCO or PURASPEC drums on a loading floor? If so, the headline matters less than the date. The four months between now and August are when the seams of the integration are widest. And they overlap with the planning window for every reformer, hydrogen plant, methanol unit, ammonia loop, and refinery hydroprocessor that has a 2026 or 2027 changeout on the calendar.

Here is what is actually changing, what isn't, and the five things worth verifying before close.

## What the Two Companies Actually Said

Johnson Matthey's transaction update is unusually candid for a public company filing. It blames the lower price on "the deferral of key sustainable solutions licensing projects and reduced profitability from the supply of catalysts due to the challenging market environment." In plain terms: 2025/26 was a slower year for new catalyst orders, and for licensing new methanol, hydrogen, and ammonia plants, than the May 2025 valuation had assumed.

JM also said it is still "on track to deliver 2025/26 performance in line with guidance," with mid single-digit underlying operating profit growth and materially higher positive free cash flow than the year before. Of the £1.325 billion it gets, JM plans to hand roughly £1 billion back to shareholders — £800 million as a special dividend with share consolidation, and £200 million as an on-market buyback.

Honeywell's release, paraphrased throughout, frames the acquisition as expanding "Honeywell UOP's capabilities with the addition of significant installed base across refining, petrochemical and renewable fuels." The release commits both parties to continue "pre-existing commercial collaborations" during the transition period — a nod to the Honeywell UOP and Johnson Matthey joint announcement from 2024 on cutting costs and speeding up sustainable aviation fuel deployment, which was already running before this acquisition was floated. There are no published commitments on staff retention, named technical-service people, or how the technology transfer will work.

That gap — what is named publicly versus what is not — is the operational reality contractor crews need to plan around.

## What Johnson Matthey Catalyst Technologies Actually Is to a Crew

If you have only ever loaded refining hydroprocessing catalyst, the JM Catalyst Technologies portfolio may not be on your daily mental map. It should be.

Johnson Matthey publicly states it is "the number one player in the world" in syngas — and that "about 40% of all the chemicals in the world" are produced through that route. The Catalyst Technologies business covers a lot of ground: steam methane reforming catalysts (the largest SMR plants in the world run JM-branded reformer tubes), pre-reforming catalysts (the CRG family, in service since the 1960s), methanol synthesis catalysts (where JM is the world's leading supplier of the catalyst that makes the molecule), ammonia synthesis catalysts and licensed technology, hydrogen production catalysts including ammonia-cracking for the emerging mobility and power markets, and the KATALCO and PURASPEC families that handle syngas purification — chloride removal, sulphur absorption, mercury removal, and the absorbents that keep ammonia and methanol loops on-spec.

For a contractor crew, here is what that adds up to. JM-supplied catalyst sits inside reformers, pre-reformers, methanators, low-temperature shift converters, methanol synthesis loops, ammonia converters, hydrogen plant guard beds, and a long tail of purification beds inside refineries. None of that is changing on August 31, 2026. The catalyst inside the vessel is the same. The bed profile, the loading procedure, the choice between sock loading and dense loading, the inerting plan, the breakthrough criteria — all the same.

What is changing is the entity on the invoice. The entity that signs the technical-service agreement. The entity whose representative shows up on day one of the loading shift. And over the next 12 to 24 months, possibly the catalyst code itself, once Honeywell UOP finishes tidying up its product line.

## What the Induction Deck Doesn't Cover

Catalyst loading inductions teach the things that kill people: nitrogen inerting, oxygen monitoring, BA discipline, dense loading vibration management, drop-test integrity, gas-tight reactor head reseal. Every SCC/VCA refresher drills it. But nothing in those decks tells a crew lead what to do when the supplier — whose technical advisor stood next to them at the manway for six previous turnarounds — is in the middle of a corporate carve-out.

Three things the training does not cover, and that the next four months will surface for any team running a 2026/27 catalyst changeout.

**1. Mid-acquisition technical-service personnel are a moving target.** The technical advisor at the manway is the person who tells the loading contractor when to slow the sock, when to break for a bed level reading, when to call the run-up. Historically, that person carried a JM business card, with JM's loading procedure in their hand and a JM lab phone number for any sample escalation. Through the integration window, that person may stay, may move onto Honeywell payroll, or may be swapped for a UOP-side advisor who does not know your specific catalyst code. None of that is published. None of it shows up on the loading day-of-job pack. And the crew lead who finds out at the manway has no leverage left to push back.

**2. Catalyst-code continuity is a paper question that becomes a field question.** A KATALCO 23-4 or a KATALCO 11-4 has decades of operating history under that exact identifier. The plant's process safety information, the unit's spec sheet, the procurement system's part number, the regen contractor's spent-catalyst paperwork, the precious metals reclaim contract — all of it points to the catalyst by its JM code. Honeywell UOP's own catalyst naming is different. Whatever mapping table the integration team produces, it will arrive after the acquisition closes, and likely after several rounds of customer questions. So a turnaround scheduled for spring 2027, with material ordered today, should be specified by both the JM code *and* the underlying technical specification — not just the trade name. That way a substitution conversation in 2026 doesn't turn into a re-qualification fight in early 2027.

**3. Spent-catalyst flow has counterparty risk that no one talks about.** Refinery and ammonia plants regularly keep rotational catalyst inventory at the supplier's regen facility — physical drums, owned by the operator, sitting on the supplier's floor between cycles. Spent catalyst with precious metal content (platinum-group reforming catalyst especially) flows back to the supplier's reclaim circuit under contracts that name the supplier entity, the supplier's facility, and the supplier's sampling protocol. M&A integration usually does not break those contracts. But it does change the entity counterparty, the address on the bills of lading, and the chain-of-custody paperwork. So a turnaround in late 2026 or 2027 that ships spent material into the new entity needs the chain-of-custody documentation re-papered before the truck leaves site — not after.

## Five Things to Verify in the Next 90 Days

For any site with a JM-supplied unit on the catalyst calendar between now and 2028:

1. **Identify every JM catalyst code in the unit by part number, vessel, and current age.** Pull the procurement records, not just the unit's process safety information. Confirm what is fresh, what is rotational inventory, and what is sitting at the supplier's regen facility.

2. **Get the current technical service contact in writing.** Ask your JM account manager for the named technical advisor assigned to your next loading event, and the escalation chain above them. Ask what changes after close. A vague answer now is a real answer.

3. **Re-document the catalyst spec.** For every JM catalyst code in your turnaround scope, attach the technical data sheet — particle size distribution, crush strength, surface area, active-metal loading where applicable — to the procurement record alongside the trade name. If the trade name is replaced post-close, the spec is what re-qualifies the substitute.

4. **Re-paper the spent-catalyst chain of custody.** Confirm with your regen and reclaim contracts which entity is the counterparty after close, where the receiving facility is, and which sampling protocol applies. If you ship spent material in September 2026 under paperwork that names the wrong entity, the dispute is yours to clean up.

5. **Update the catalyst loading day-of-job pack to reference both supplier identities.** If the technical advisor at the manway introduces themselves with a UOP business card and a JM loading procedure, the crew lead should be able to point to a single document that anticipates that moment. Ambiguity at the reactor head is what creates loading errors.

## The One Thing Not to Do

Do not let a procurement office substitute "equivalent" catalyst between vendors during the integration window without a formal re-qualification. UOP catalyst and JM catalyst do many of the same jobs, but they are not interchangeable at the level of bed depth, pressure drop, breakthrough behaviour, or precious-metal loading. A purchase order that quietly switches part numbers because "the new owner makes that catalyst now" can leave you with a bed that is 5 millimetres taller than the dense-load curve assumed, or a guard bed that breaks through three months earlier than the operating envelope predicted. The integration team will publish an official mapping. Wait for it.

## What This Means for European Crews

Honeywell UOP and Johnson Matthey both have deep European installed bases. Most refineries on the Continent run JM-supplied catalysts somewhere in the hydrogen, methanol, ammonia, or syngas chain. SCC/VCA-certified loading crews working on those units will see the change land first as a logo swap on the technical advisor's hi-vis vest, then as a renamed catalog of catalyst codes over the following 12 to 24 months. The work itself — the inert entry, the bed profile, the dense-load vibration, the post-load drop test — does not change. The paperwork around it will. And the crew lead who has the documentation ready before the integration team arrives will run a quieter turnaround than the one who finds out at the manway.

For young technicians weighing a career in catalyst loading: this is also the kind of industry event that does not appear in any textbook. Catalyst supplier consolidation is one of those slow forces that quietly reshapes who pays your day-rate, who writes your loading procedure, and which catalyst families you spend your career learning. The supplier list is not as long as it looks. Two of the four names that come up in every refinery technical procurement conversation just became one.
