A refinery turnaround is the most operationally and financially significant event a plant can undergo. Done right, it extends asset life, restores throughput capacity, and keeps the unit running safely until the next planned shutdown. Done wrong, it costs the refinery millions per day in extended downtime and can leave equipment in worse condition than before it started.

We have been part of turnarounds across Europe — at Shell, ExxonMobil, BP, Neste, and ORLEN facilities — and what we have learned is that the difference between a well-executed and a poorly executed turnaround almost always comes down to preparation, not what happens during the execution window itself.

## What Is a Refinery Turnaround?

A turnaround is a planned, temporary shutdown of a refinery process unit for inspection, maintenance, repair, and modification work that cannot be done while the unit is online. Turnarounds are typically scheduled every four to six years, though some units require more frequent attention depending on process conditions and the regulator's inspection intervals.

The scale varies enormously. A single-unit TA might involve a crew of 50 over three weeks. A full refinery turnaround can mobilize thousands of workers across dozens of contractors simultaneously — coordinating scaffolding, inspection, catalyst changeout, mechanical repairs, electrical work, and recommissioning in parallel.

## Phase 1: Scope Development

The turnaround starts not with a wrench, but with a list. Scope development begins 18 to 24 months before the planned shutdown date. This is where the refinery's maintenance and operations teams compile every work order that requires the unit to be offline — from reactor internals inspections to refractory lining repairs to valve replacements.

Getting the scope right is everything. Add too much and you extend the shutdown unnecessarily. Miss critical items and you find yourself negotiating emergency scope additions mid-turnaround, which destroys the schedule and the budget simultaneously.

We get involved in scope review early, particularly for reactor work. Our role at this stage is to assess what inspection and catalyst work is realistically achievable within the planned windows, and flag items where access or tooling requirements are likely to cause delays if not addressed in advance.

## Phase 2: Planning and Resource Mobilization

Once scope is locked — or as locked as it ever gets — the planning phase begins in earnest. This covers several parallel workstreams:

**Work pack development.** Every job gets a detailed procedure, risk assessment, and materials list before any boots are on the ground. A work pack that is poorly written in the office becomes a safety incident or a schedule delay in the field.

**Scaffold design.** Complex reactors and heat exchangers require engineered scaffold solutions, sometimes with specialist structural support calculations. These cannot be improvised on the day of entry.

**Critical path analysis.** The TA scheduler identifies which jobs must complete before others can start. Reactor entry cannot begin until purge and confirmed inert conditions are in place. First catalyst cannot be loaded until the bed support is verified as intact and correctly installed.

**Contractor coordination.** Multiple crews from different disciplines — mechanical, inspection, catalyst, scaffolding, electrical — must be sequenced without creating bottlenecks or overlapping work zones that compromise safety or access.

For our catalyst and reactor internals work, we coordinate closely with the refinery's inspection team and the catalyst vendor's technical representative from this phase forward. Pre-TA loading plans are drafted, reviewed, and revised before we ever open a manway.

## Phase 3: Execution — Where the Plan Meets Reality

No turnaround executes exactly to plan. Equipment is almost always in worse condition than inspection records suggest. A reactor that was supposed to take three days to unload takes four because the spent catalyst is more consolidated than expected. A scaffold modification delays first entry by half a shift. Unforecast rain stops outdoor work.

What separates experienced TA crews from inexperienced ones is the ability to adapt without losing the thread of the critical path. We have worked in conditions ranging from Bulgarian winter to Finnish summer, and the discipline we bring to daily progress meetings and schedule updates is what keeps a job recoverable when it starts to drift.

**Morning standups are non-negotiable.** Every supervisor confirms overnight progress, flags current obstacles, and commits to the day's deliverables before the day shift begins. If the critical path is threatened, it is far better to know at 07:00 than at 17:00.

**Permit discipline prevents incidents.** In a busy turnaround, there can be hundreds of active permits across a single unit. Every confined space entry, every hot work operation, every energy isolation must be correctly documented and authorized before work begins. Permit-to-work discipline is the difference between a safe turnaround and a fatal one.

**Catalyst work follows written procedures, always.** We do not improvise catalyst loading. Every step — inerting, vessel entry, bed support verification, catalyst loading sequence, dense loading parameters — follows the pre-approved written procedure. Any deviation requires supervisor sign-off before work continues.

## Phase 4: Recommissioning and Startup

Mechanical completion is not the end of a turnaround. It is the beginning of the highest-risk phase. Recommissioning involves reintroducing hydrocarbons to equipment that has been opened, cleaned, and reassembled by hundreds of hands. A missed blind, an improperly torqued flange, or a valve left in the wrong position can turn a successful shutdown into a serious process safety incident.

We remain on-site through catalyst reduction and activation for any reactors we have loaded. Watching the first heatup, monitoring bed temperature profiles, and being available to advise on anomalies is part of what we provide — not an optional extra.
