A refinery turnaround is the single biggest event a plant can go through — both operationally and financially. Get it right, and you extend the asset's life, restore how much it can produce, and keep the unit running safely until the next planned shutdown. Get it wrong, and it costs the refinery millions a day in extra downtime — and can leave equipment in worse shape than before you started.

We have been part of turnarounds across Europe — at Shell, ExxonMobil, BP, Neste, and ORLEN facilities — and here is what we have learned. The difference between a turnaround that goes well and one that goes badly almost always comes down to preparation, not to what happens during the execution window itself.

## What Is a Refinery Turnaround?

A turnaround is a planned, temporary shutdown of a refinery process unit. You take the unit offline to do the inspection, maintenance, repair, and modification work that simply cannot be done while it is running. Turnarounds usually happen every four to six years, though some units need attention more often, depending on what they process and how often the regulator requires an inspection.

The scale swings enormously. A single-unit TA might be a crew of 50 over three weeks. A full refinery turnaround can pull in thousands of workers across dozens of contractors all at once — coordinating scaffolding, inspection, catalyst changeout, mechanical repairs, electrical work, and recommissioning, all in parallel.

## Phase 1: Scope Development

A turnaround starts not with a wrench, but with a list. Scope development begins 18 to 24 months before the planned shutdown date. This is where the refinery's maintenance and operations teams gather up every work order that needs the unit offline — everything from reactor internals inspections to refractory lining repairs to valve replacements.

Getting the scope right is everything. Add too much, and you drag out the shutdown for no reason. Miss something critical, and you end up negotiating emergency scope additions in the middle of the turnaround — which wrecks the schedule and the budget at the same time.

We get involved in scope review early, particularly for reactor work. Our job at this stage is to look at the inspection and catalyst work and judge what can realistically be done in the planned windows — and to flag anything where access or tooling is likely to cause delays if it is not sorted out in advance.

## Phase 2: Planning and Resource Mobilization

Once the scope is locked — or as locked as it ever really gets — planning begins in earnest. Several workstreams run side by side:

**Work pack development.** Every job gets a detailed procedure, a risk assessment, and a materials list before anyone sets foot on the ground. A work pack written badly in the office becomes a safety incident or a schedule delay out in the field.

**Scaffold design.** Complex reactors and heat exchangers need engineered scaffold solutions — sometimes with specialist structural support calculations. You cannot improvise these on the day you need to climb in.

**Critical path analysis.** The TA scheduler works out which jobs have to finish before others can start. Reactor entry cannot begin until the unit is purged and inert conditions are confirmed. The first catalyst cannot go in until the bed support is verified as intact and correctly installed.

**Contractor coordination.** Multiple crews from different trades — mechanical, inspection, catalyst, scaffolding, electrical — have to be sequenced so they do not create bottlenecks or end up working on top of each other in ways that compromise safety or access.

For our catalyst and reactor internals work, we work closely with the refinery's inspection team and the catalyst vendor's technical representative from this phase onward. Pre-TA loading plans get drafted, reviewed, and revised long before we ever open a manway.

## Phase 3: Execution — Where the Plan Meets Reality

No turnaround runs exactly to plan. The equipment is almost always in worse condition than the inspection records suggested. A reactor that was supposed to take three days to unload takes four, because the spent catalyst is more packed-together than expected. A scaffold modification pushes first entry back by half a shift. Rain nobody forecast stops the outdoor work.

What separates experienced TA crews from inexperienced ones is the ability to adapt without losing the thread of the critical path. We have worked in everything from Bulgarian winter to Finnish summer, and the discipline we bring to the daily progress meetings and schedule updates is what keeps a job recoverable when it starts to drift.

**Morning standups are non-negotiable.** Every supervisor confirms overnight progress, flags the obstacles in front of them, and commits to the day's deliverables before the day shift starts. If the critical path is under threat, it is far better to know at 07:00 than at 17:00.

**Permit discipline prevents incidents.** In a busy turnaround, a single unit can have hundreds of active permits at once. Every confined space entry, every hot work operation, every energy isolation has to be correctly documented and authorized before work begins. Permit-to-work discipline is the difference between a safe turnaround and a fatal one.

**Catalyst work follows written procedures, always.** We do not improvise catalyst loading. Every step — inerting, vessel entry, bed support verification, catalyst loading sequence, dense loading parameters — follows the pre-approved written procedure. Any deviation needs supervisor sign-off before work goes on.

## Phase 4: Recommissioning and Startup

Mechanical completion is not the end of a turnaround. It is the start of its highest-risk phase. Recommissioning means reintroducing hydrocarbons into equipment that has just been opened, cleaned, and reassembled by hundreds of hands. A missed blind, a flange that was not torqued properly, or a valve left in the wrong position can turn a successful shutdown into a serious process safety incident.

We stay on-site through catalyst reduction and activation for any reactors we have loaded. Watching the first heatup, monitoring the bed temperature profiles, and being there to advise on anything unusual is part of what we provide — not an optional extra.
