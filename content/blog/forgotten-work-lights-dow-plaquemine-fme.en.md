In May 2023, a maintenance crew climbed out of a reflux drum at Dow's Glycol II Ethylene Oxide Finishing unit in Plaquemine, Louisiana. (A reflux drum is a vessel that holds liquid and vapour during normal running.) It had been opened for a routine turnaround — a planned shutdown for maintenance. Someone signed the closure form. The unit was buttoned up and put back into service.

Eight weeks later, on the night of July 14, a series of explosions tore through the unit and released more than 31,000 pounds of ethylene oxide into the air. A shelter-in-place order went out to hundreds of nearby residents. The CSB's final report, [released on 26 February 2026](https://www.csb.gov/us-chemical-safety-board-releases-investigation-report-on-the-2023-explosion-and-toxic-ethylene-oxide-release-at-dow-plant-in-plaquemine-louisiana/), traces the whole thing back to one small detail: portable work lights left inside the drum.

This is a post about what the closure form didn't say.

## What actually happened

The CSB report walks through the chain of failures in detail. We'll give the short version, then sit with the part contractors will recognise.

In May 2023, Dow shut the Glycol II unit for planned maintenance. Workers went into a reflux drum — the vessel that holds liquid and vapour ethylene oxide during normal operation — to do internal work. When the job was done, the drum was closed and the unit restarted. Two portable work lights were left inside. Over the following weeks, they fell apart. Bits of metal debris from the lights worked their way through the drum and eventually punched a hole in a rupture disc on the pressure-relief piping. (A rupture disc is a thin metal seal designed to burst at a set pressure — here it acted as the wall between the process and the relief piping.)

That's the moment everything else followed from. With the disc breached, ethylene oxide started bleeding into the relief header — about 50 feet of pipe that was supposed to be filled with nitrogen ("inerted"), so that any process leak stayed below the level where it could catch fire.

It wasn't. The nitrogen had quietly leaked away over time. Air had filled the piping instead. Ethylene oxide flowed in, mixed with the oxygen in that air, found a spark, and the flame raced the full 50 feet back to the relief valve, lifted it, and pushed into the reflux drum's vapour space. Ethylene oxide can break apart explosively once it's lit. The drum failed catastrophically. More explosions followed.

Two failures, one after the other, and neither one alone would have been a disaster. The lights would have stayed harmless if the inerting had been real. The inerting failure would have stayed harmless if the disc had been intact. Process safety almost always works that way — it takes two holes lining up.

CSB Chairperson Steve Owens summed up the human side in plain language: *"The workers did not remove all the work lights from inside the drum, and Dow did not have an effective procedure in place to ensure that they did so."*

## The closure form was a checkbox

The detail that belongs on every turnaround manager's desk is buried in the report. Dow's Vessel/Nozzle Closure Form — the document that authorised closing the drum — *"provided no guidance on how to check for debris or ensure clean and free of debris; it functioned as a checklist only."*

Read that twice. The form had a box. Someone ticked the box. The box never said *how* to make sure the inside of a vessel was actually clean. There was no instruction to use a mirror, a borescope (a small camera on a flexible cable), or even a structured walk-around. The standard practice on the day was a flashlight beam through the manway — sometimes from a single angle, by whoever happened to be the last person looking in.

Anyone who's worked turnarounds knows exactly what this looks like. The shift is ending. The vessel has had four trades through it in the last ten days — entry crew, mechanical, NDT, blasting. The job pack lists the manway gasket and the bolt-up torque. It doesn't list how many tools went in versus how many came out. The permit writer is two units over, closing another job. The standby man is making a coffee. The closure form is on the desk. *Vessel inspected — clean. Initials. Time-stamp.* Box ticked.

A flashlight beam through a manway, into a vessel full of internals casting shadows, is not an inspection. It's a hope.

## Where the contractor sits in this

The CSB makes a point that lands hard for anyone running a contracting crew. Investigators noted that *"contractors were frequently the last in the vessel"* — with no clear owner of the final cleanup. Multiple trades cycled through the drum during the May 2023 work. When the last person left, who confirmed what was inside? The report couldn't draw a clean line.

We've seen this exact pattern on every turnaround we've worked. The rotation of trades inside a vessel is rarely documented down to the level of "who signed the inside off as empty." The mechanical crew leaves and assumes the next entrant will pick up anything they missed. The NDT contractor goes in, does their pass, leaves. The cleaning crew comes through and may or may not be told about loose hardware from earlier work. The closure form treats the vessel as one object that was either clean or not at a single moment — not as a vessel that twenty people have entered and exited over a week.

Foreign material exclusion — FME, in turbomachinery and confined-space terms — is the discipline that closes that gap. On nuclear and aerospace work, FME is treated like a religion: every tool tagged, every entry logged, tool counts reconciled in and out, an FME monitor posted at the manway. On petrochemical turnarounds it's uneven. Some operators run it tightly. Plenty don't.

Dow's response after the incident was to create what they call a *Global Foreign Materials Exclusion Standard*, covering tool inventory and final inspection criteria. That's the right corrective action. The unsettling part is that a Tier-1 operator with a serious process-safety programme didn't already have one.

## What the standard procedure should look like

This is the part the training card doesn't cover. The card teaches gas testing, atmosphere monitoring, retrieval rigs, communications. It doesn't teach the boring admin ritual that actually keeps work lights out of vessels. On a tightly run job — and this is what a crew lead can ask for on Monday morning — the standard procedure has four pieces.

**A pre-entry tool log.** Before the entry begins, every tool, every light, every rag, every soft good gets listed on a sheet posted at the manway. Not described — listed, by count, and by serial number where it applies. Two LED work lights, model X, serial Y, Y+1. Three 1/2-inch socket sets. Six mechanic's rags. The list grows whenever a tool is added during the shift. This is fifteen minutes of admin, and it's the backbone of everything that follows.

**A reconciliation at exit.** Every time a person comes out, the list gets reviewed. Every time the entry closes for the shift, the list gets reconciled — items in equals items out, and anything still inside is recorded as still inside. The standby man owns this list. Not the entry supervisor, not the permit writer. The standby — because they're the only person whose entire job is to be at the manway.

**A pre-closure walk by a nominated person.** Before the manway gets bolted up, one named person — the entry supervisor on most crews — does a final inside check with a mirror or borescope, not a flashlight held at arm's length. That person signs the closure form. Their signature is the claim that nothing was left inside. If they weren't the last entrant, they go in for the check. There's no version of "well, the last guy was in there an hour ago and said it was fine."

**A post-closure verification with the operator.** The contractor's closure doesn't become final until the unit operator countersigns that they understood what the closure form said. This is the seam where ownership changes hands, and it's where things get dropped. NFPA's confined-space-entry guidance — and the related ASSP standard for entering confined spaces — both treat the closure as a documented hand-off, not a tickbox. Dow's old form didn't.

If your turnaround pack doesn't include all four pieces, the gap isn't academic. It's the gap that wrote 47 pages of CSB report.

## The lesson for crew leads

Three things to take into your next pre-job briefing.

First, ask to see the closure form before the job starts, not on the last day. If it's a tickbox sheet rather than a verification protocol, raise it. The time to push back on a Vessel/Nozzle Closure Form that doesn't spell out *how* the vessel gets checked is during planning, when there's room to write the procedure differently. By the time the closure is actually happening, schedule pressure has eaten everything.

Second, run the FME log even if the operator doesn't ask for it. Crews with SCC/VCA training already drill confined-space entry and gas testing — the FME log is the next discipline up. It's a clipboard, a pen, and a standby man who writes things down. It doesn't need new equipment or a software system. It needs a crew lead who treats the count as a non-negotiable part of the entry, the same way they treat the gas test.

Third, accept that you'll be the last person in the vessel. Contracting crews rotate, but on most turnarounds the final pre-closure entry is a contractor. That puts the FME claim on you. The CSB's wording about "contractors were frequently the last in the vessel" wasn't blame — it was a description of where the procedural seam fell. Knowing you sit on that seam is the difference between a clean closure and a tickbox.

The Plaquemine drum exploded because two failures lined up. One of them was an inerting system the operator owned. The other was a closure form that anyone on the manway could have caught and pushed back on. The operator owns the inerting. The closure is shared.
