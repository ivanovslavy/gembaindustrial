In May 2026, Topsoe published a customer case that has been making the rounds on procurement desks and refinery-margin spreadsheets. A European refinery, kept anonymous in the write-up, swapped the catalyst in its mild hydrocracker over to Topsoe's TK-939 D-sel™ — even though the catalyst it was already running was doing fine.

The payoff, in Topsoe's own numbers, was big. Middle distillate yield up 4 wt% across an 18-month cycle. Cloud point down 5 to 8°C. An extra 450,000 barrels of diesel. And US$7.6 million in additional profit per catalyst cycle.

Yes, the case is a marketing document, and it reads like one. But the decision behind it is real, and operators are copying it right now — because diesel is doing the heavy lifting in their 2026 margin model, and this catalyst makes more of it.

So why should *you* care? If you are a turnaround planner or a catalyst-changeout crew lead, the headline profit number is not the interesting part. The interesting part is everything the case study quietly assumes about the unit, the cycle, and the people who load and dump the catalyst — most of which never made it into the marketing copy. Here is the longer reading.

## What Topsoe Actually Said

Topsoe describes TK-939 D-sel as "a low zeolitic NiW catalyst with high selectivity for middle distillates." In plain terms: it is nickel and tungsten sitting on a support, with a small amount of zeolite (a crystalline material that helps crack big molecules). It is built to crack vacuum gas oil and similar heavy feeds into diesel-range molecules — without overcracking them all the way down into naphtha (a lighter, lower-value product).

Topsoe puts it in the same D-sel™ family as TK-949 and TK-969 — the diesel-selectivity products — as opposed to the higher-activity grades that make more naphtha. An earlier *PTQ* technical article by Hansen and Andersson (2017) walked through the single-stage and two-stage trade-offs in detail.

The European case that the May 2026 announcement is built on adds the field numbers. Even though the refinery was happy with the catalyst it had, Topsoe advised switching to TK-939 D-sel to chase more middle distillate. Over an 18-month cycle, the unit made an extra 450,000 barrels of diesel — roughly 830 barrels a day above where it started — at a cloud point 5 to 8°C lower than the old catalyst gave. Topsoe puts the profit gain for the whole cycle at $7.6 million.

Here is what is *not* in the case study, and it is exactly what matters for the people working the next turnaround: the hydrocracker type (single-stage or two-stage), the feedstock split (VGO, coker gas oil, deasphalted oil, recycle), the pressure envelope, whether the old catalyst was also NiW or whether the unit moved over from NiMo, and how the fresh charge gets loaded. Every one of those is something the catalyst-changeout crew inherits.

## Why a Crew Lead Should Read a Margin Case Study

Here is the mistake catalyst-changeout crews make — and young technicians on their first hydrocracker turnaround make it most. They treat the catalyst in the reactor as a fixed feature of the unit. It is not. Catalyst families change from one cycle to the next, and which family they pick changes what the dump crew finds when the head comes off eighteen months later.

The chemistry is not interchangeable at the unloading face. A NiW catalyst at end of run is not the same as a NiMo catalyst at end of run. Both are sulphided. Both are pyrophoric — meaning they can self-heat and catch fire in air — once the protective sulphide layer is broken. Both demand inert entry under nitrogen. But how fast they self-heat in air, how the temperature behaves during passivation, and what the bed surface looks like when you get inside all differ between the two families.

The bed the case study implies is a hydrocracking bed, not a hydrotreating bed. Picture it as a stack: multiple beds, with quench gas distributors, ceramic ball supports, graded inert layers, scale baskets, and an active main bed. A switch to a D-sel grade usually comes with a Topsoe-recommended loading scheme that re-proportions the bed depths, the inert layers, and the support layers compared to the old charge. If procurement updated the product name but not the loading drawing, the crew on the floor is the one who finds the mismatch — at the manway.

And the cycle length — eighteen months — quietly sets how hard the next dump will be. A catalyst that runs hard for eighteen months builds up a coke skin on top of the bed, and the crew has to break that skin before the bulk can flow out. How thick that skin is, how tightly it bonds to the inert layer on top, and how much heavy residue it holds are all downstream consequences of a run that procurement signed up for back at the *previous* turnaround.

## What Changes Inside the Reactor

NiW catalysts run on the same first principles as NiMo — sulphide-form metals on a heat-resistant support, hydrogen-rich service, sour feed, high temperature, high pressure — but the operating window is different in ways that matter at the bed. NiW is the chemistry of choice for high-pressure hydrocracking pretreatment, and for the cracking-active bed in mild hydrocrackers, because tungsten holds its activity at higher temperature than molybdenum and tolerates higher hydrogen partial pressure without sintering (clumping together and losing surface area). The trade-off: NiW formulations are denser and harder to sulphide.

The sulphiding ramp uses DMDS or similar agents on a controlled temperature curve, with a window that sits a little higher than for NiMo. None of that is the crew's job — operations runs the sulphiding step — but here is why it reaches the floor anyway. A bed that was sulphided to spec and run for eighteen months ends up in a deeper sulphide state at end of run than a less aggressively sulphided NiMo bed. So its pyrophoric reactivity in air is more sensitive to temperature spikes during passivation. In short: it can catch you out faster.

The case study's 4 wt% middle-distillate gain at the same cycle length means one of two things — either an operating point tuned for selectivity, or a Topsoe loading scheme that adjusted bed depths to keep severity in check. A selectivity-skewed run, all else equal, lays down less of the heavy polyaromatic naphthalenes (HPNAs) at the bottom of the bed that foul the lower beds, and more of the top-of-bed coke that comes from fouling at the feed end. In other words, the end-of-run signature shifts *up* the reactor. A crew expecting the heaviest skull at the bottom may instead find the heaviest skinning at the top of the cracking bed — several metres above where the unloader nozzle was originally rigged.

## End of Run: What the Crew Inherits

Most of the catalyst-changeout briefing on a hydrocracker is about inerted entry, BA discipline, and unloader rigging. Those are the parts the SCC/VCA refresher card covers in detail. What the card does not cover is how to read the bed once the team is inside.

With the head off, the standby man and the crew lead are working from indirect cues — they can't see the whole bed, so they go by signs. The dump rate the unloader nozzle pulls. The resistance the vacuum hose meets. The colour of the catalyst coming through the screen at the bagging station. The temperature of the bagged material in the IBC. On a long-cycle, selectivity-skewed run, the expected sequence goes like this: a relatively clean top layer (inert balls and grading), then a hard skin (coked top of the active bed, maybe with a polymeric film over it), then a longer-than-usual stretch of well-preserved active catalyst, then HPNA-bearing fines at the bottom that clog the screens.

Inert entry — the standby-man-and-entrant rotation, the constant nitrogen sweep, the oxygen monitoring at the entrant's lapel — is the same procedure every time. What changes between cycles is how long it takes. A skinned bed takes longer to break than a clean one. Longer breaking means more inert-entry hours, which puts pressure on the BA shift rotation and the standby cylinder swap-out cycle. A schedule built off the *previous* cycle's dump time, with no adjustment for the skinning profile of a D-sel run, will run long. And crews that run long under nitrogen on a tight BA bottle plan are exactly the crews that start taking shortcuts on the relief.

The pyrophoricity passivation step at the end of the unload follows the same logic. A more deeply sulphided NiW bed at end of run can give a sharper exotherm — a sudden burst of heat — the moment air first reaches the residual catalyst dust on the internals. Operations sets the air-introduction rate, but it is the crew lead's read of the local skin TCs (thermocouples — the temperature sensors on the reactor wall) that decides whether the inspection crew waits another hour or goes in. Read it like a previous-cycle NiMo passivation pattern, then apply that to an NiW residue, and you can put inspectors at the manway too early.

## Loading the Fresh D-sel Charge

A D-sel charge for a refinery chasing the yield gain the case study advertises is almost always dense-loaded in the active bed. Dense loading packs more active surface area into a fixed reactor volume, and that is what makes the yield numbers possible. It also takes substantially longer than sock loading, needs a different vibrating tool, and runs from a different procedure document.

For a crew that has never worked the unit before, the day-of-job pack is the single most important document there is. The pack should reference the Topsoe loading procedure by document number, the bed-by-bed scheme, the catalyst codes by drum, the inert load (grading and balls), the scale-basket configuration, the maximum drop height, the vibration time and amplitude, the bed-level verification frequency, the unload route for off-spec material, and the post-load drop test method. A pack that names only the catalyst and the bed depth is missing two-thirds of what the loading shift actually needs.

One small detail that bites in practice: catalyst drums for D-sel and other selective grades sometimes ship with a different barrel labelling convention than the old catalyst — same supplier, different product line, different code stamping. Procurement systems that take catalyst names at face value can stage the wrong drums to the loading floor. And the crew lead at the manway, looking at a drum that does not match the pack, has no room left to push back once the shift has already started.

## What This Means for the Schedule

The cloud-point gain a D-sel catalyst delivers is worth the most in the northern hemisphere winter-grade window — roughly Q4 and Q1. Operators who catch that cloud-point gain in a winter cycle earn a premium on the diesel price that a summer run never sees. So the *finish* date of the changeout — the moment the unit is back on stream — is a constrained variable, not a free one.

A schedule with no allowance for a heavier-than-baseline skinning event, no contingency on inert-entry duration, and no buffer on BA cylinder swap rate is a schedule that has not modelled the D-sel reality. The marketing case study puts a number on the upside. The downside stays invisible — right up until it shows up on day three of the dump.

## Five Things Worth Verifying Before the Next Hydrocracker Turnaround

For any hydrocracker on the calendar in the next twelve months where a D-sel grade is in scope on either the unload or the load:

1. **Confirm the chemistry of the catalyst coming out and the catalyst going in.** NiW out and NiW in is one job. NiMo out and NiW in is a different briefing for the dump crew and a different sulphiding plan for operations. The day-of-job pack should state both.

2. **Pull the Topsoe loading procedure document number into the day-of-job pack.** A trade name is not enough. The procedure number, the revision date, the bed-by-bed scheme, and the dense-loading vibration spec all live in a Topsoe-supplied document that the loading contractor should have on the floor before the head opens.

3. **Adjust the inert-entry duration assumption upward.** Skinned beds take longer to dump than clean beds. A schedule built off the previous cycle's dump time on a different catalyst is not a schedule for this one.

4. **Stage the BA cylinder swap plan for the worst-case dump duration, not the expected one.** Pressure on the BA bottles is what creates the short-cuts. The plan should be conservative.

5. **Watch the air-purge passivation TC trace personally.** The panel reads what the panel reads. The crew lead's eyes on the local skin temperatures during the air introduction are what catch a sharper exotherm than the previous-cycle baseline predicted.

For young technicians weighing a future in hydrocracker catalyst-changeout work, here is the bigger picture. The same forces that decide the catalyst inside the reactor — diesel margin, crack spread, winter-grade premium, supplier roadmap — also decide the work the crew sees at the next turnaround. The bed never picks itself. Someone in a procurement meeting six months before the changeout picked it. The crew lead's job is to know which case study they are working inside.
