Topsoe published a customer case in May 2026 that has been making the rounds on procurement desks and refinery-margin spreadsheets. A European refinery, anonymised in the write-up, swapped its mild hydrocracker into Topsoe's TK-939 D-sel™ catalyst even though the prior charge was performing to expectation. The result, in Topsoe's own numbers: middle distillate yield up 4 wt% across an 18-month cycle, cloud point down 5 to 8°C, an extra 450,000 barrels of diesel, and US$7.6 million in additional profit per catalyst cycle. The case is a marketing document and reads like one, but the underlying decision is real, and it is being copied right now by operators who have noticed that the diesel crack spread is doing the heavy lifting in their 2026 margin model.

If you are a turnaround planner or a catalyst-changeout crew lead, the headline number is not the interesting bit. The interesting bit is what the case study assumes about the unit, the cycle, and the people who load and dump the catalyst — most of which is not in the marketing copy. Here is the longer reading.

## What Topsoe Actually Said

TK-939 D-sel is described by Topsoe as "a low zeolitic NiW catalyst with high selectivity for middle distillates." Unpacked: nickel and tungsten on a support with a small fraction of zeolite, designed to crack vacuum gas oil and similar feeds into diesel-range hydrocarbons without overcracking into naphtha. Topsoe positions it alongside TK-949 and TK-969 in the D-sel™ family — the diesel-selectivity play — versus the higher-activity grades that yield more naphtha. The earlier *PTQ* technical article by Hansen and Andersson (2017) covered the single-stage and two-stage trade-offs in detail.

The European case the May 2026 announcement is built on adds the field numbers. Even though the refinery was satisfied with the prior charge, Topsoe advised a switch into TK-939 D-sel to chase higher middle-distillate yield. Over an 18-month cycle the unit produced an extra 450,000 barrels of diesel — about 830 barrels per day above baseline — at a cloud point 5 to 8°C lower than the prior catalyst delivered. Topsoe puts the cycle-level profit gain at $7.6 million.

What is *not* in the case study, and what matters for the people who work the next turnaround: the hydrocracker type (single-stage or two-stage), the feedstock split (VGO, coker gas oil, deasphalted oil, recycle), the pressure envelope, whether the prior catalyst was also NiW or whether the unit moved from NiMo, and the loading method on the fresh charge. Each of those is what the catalyst-changeout crew inherits.

## Why a Crew Lead Should Read a Margin Case Study

The mistake catalyst-changeout crews make — particularly young technicians taking their first turnaround on a hydrocracker — is treating the catalyst grade in the reactor as a fixed feature of the unit. It is not. Catalyst families shift between cycles, and the choice of family changes what the dump crew finds when the head comes off eighteen months later.

The chemistry is not interchangeable at the unloading face. A NiW catalyst at end of run is not a NiMo catalyst at end of run. Both are sulphided, both are pyrophoric in air once the protective sulphide layer is breached, both demand inert entry under nitrogen. But the rate of self-heating in air, the temperature profile during passivation, and the visual cues at the bed surface differ between the two families.

The bed geometry the case study implies is a hydrocracking bed, not a hydrotreating bed: multi-bed, with quench gas distributors, ceramic ball supports, graded inert layers, scale baskets, and an active main bed. A switch into a D-sel grade typically comes with a Topsoe-recommended loading scheme that re-proportions bed depths, inert layers, and support layers relative to the prior charge. If the procurement system updated the trade name but not the loading drawing, the crew on the floor finds the discrepancy at the manway.

The cycle length — eighteen months — quietly determines the difficulty of the dump that follows. A catalyst that runs hard for eighteen months accumulates a coke skin on the top of the bed that the crew has to break before the bulk can flow. The skin thickness, the bond to the inert top layer, and the polyaromatic content of the residue are downstream consequences of the run that procurement committed to in the previous turnaround.

## What Changes Inside the Reactor

NiW catalysts run on the same first principles as NiMo — sulphide-form metals on a refractory support, hydrogen-rich service, sour feed, high temperature, high pressure — but the operating window is different in ways that matter at the bed. NiW is the preferred chemistry for high-pressure hydrocracking pretreatment and for the cracking-active bed in mild hydrocrackers because tungsten holds its activity at higher temperature than molybdenum and tolerates higher hydrogen partial pressure without sintering. The trade-off is that NiW formulations are denser and harder to sulphide. The sulphiding ramp uses DMDS or similar agents at a controlled temperature curve, with a window that sits a little higher than for NiMo. None of that is the crew's responsibility — operations runs the sulphiding step — but a bed that was sulphided to spec and run for eighteen months sits in a deeper sulphide state at end of run than a less aggressively sulphided NiMo bed. The pyrophoric reactivity profile in air is, accordingly, more sensitive to temperature spikes during passivation.

The case study's 4 wt% middle-distillate gain at constant cycle length implies either a selectivity-favouring operating point or a Topsoe loading scheme that adjusted bed depths to keep severity in check. A selectivity-skewed run, all else equal, accumulates less of the bottom-of-bed heavy polyaromatic naphthalenes (HPNAs) that foul lower beds, and more of the top-of-bed coke that comes from feed-end fouling. The end-of-run signature shifts up the reactor. A crew that expects the heaviest skull at the bottom may find the heaviest skinning at the top of the cracking bed, several metres above where the unloader nozzle was originally rigged.

## End of Run: What the Crew Inherits

Most of the catalyst-changeout briefing on a hydrocracker focuses on inerted entry, BA discipline, and unloader rigging. Those are the bits the SCC/VCA refresher card covers in detail. What the card does not cover is the read of the bed once the team is inside.

With the head off, the standby man and the crew lead are working from indirect cues: the dump rate the unloader nozzle pulls, the resistance the vacuum hose meets, the colour of the catalyst coming through the screen at the bagging station, the temperature of bagged material in the IBC. On a long-cycle, selectivity-skewed run, the expected sequence is a relatively clean top layer (inert balls and grading), then a hard skin (coked top of the active bed, possibly with a polymeric film), then a longer than usual stretch of well-preserved active catalyst, then HPNA-bearing fines at the bottom that load the screens.

Inert entry — standby-man-and-entrant rotation, constant nitrogen sweep, oxygen monitoring at the entrant's lapel — is the same procedure every time. What changes between cycles is the duration. A skinned bed takes longer to break than a clean one, which extends the inert-entry hours, which puts pressure on BA shift rotation and the standby cylinder swap-out cycle. A schedule built off the previous cycle's dump time, with no adjustment for the skinning profile of a D-sel run, will run long. Crews that run long under nitrogen with a tight BA bottle plan are the crews that take shortcuts on the relief.

The pyrophoricity passivation step at the end of the unload follows the same logic. A more deeply sulphided NiW bed at end of run can give a sharper exotherm at the moment air first reaches residual catalyst dust on the internals. Operations sets the air-introduction rate, but the crew lead's read of the local skin TCs is what determines whether the inspection crew waits another hour or goes in. A reading that looks like a previous-cycle NiMo passivation pattern, applied to an NiW residue, can put inspectors at the manway too early.

## Loading the Fresh D-sel Charge

A D-sel grade charge for a refinery chasing the yield gain the case study advertises is almost always dense-loaded in the active bed. Dense loading puts more active surface area into a fixed reactor volume and is what makes the yield numbers achievable. It also takes substantially longer than sock loading, demands a different vibrating tool, and runs from a different procedure document.

For a crew that has not worked the unit before, the day-of-job pack is the single most important document. The pack should reference the Topsoe loading procedure by document number, the bed-by-bed scheme, the catalyst codes by drum, the inert load (grading and balls), the scale-basket configuration, the maximum drop height, the vibration time and amplitude, the bed-level verification frequency, the unload route for off-spec material, and the post-load drop test method. A pack that names only the catalyst and the bed depth is missing two-thirds of what the loading shift needs.

A small detail that comes up in practice: catalyst drums for D-sel and other selective grades sometimes ship in a different barrel labelling convention than the prior catalyst — same supplier, different product line, different code stamping. Procurement systems that take catalyst names at face value can stage the wrong drums to the loading floor. The crew lead at the manway, looking at a drum that does not match the pack, has no room left to push back if the shift has already started.

## What This Means for the Schedule

The cloud-point gain a D-sel catalyst delivers is most valuable in the northern hemisphere winter-grade window — Q4 and Q1, roughly. Operators that catch the cloud-point gain in a winter cycle add a premium to the diesel price that a summer run does not. That makes the *finish* date of the changeout — the moment the unit is back on stream — a constrained variable, not a free one. A schedule with no allowance for a heavier-than-baseline skinning event, no contingency on inert-entry duration, and no buffer on BA cylinder swap rate is a schedule that has not modelled the D-sel reality. The marketing case study quantifies the upside. The downside is qualitative until it shows up on day three of the dump.

## Five Things Worth Verifying Before the Next Hydrocracker Turnaround

For any hydrocracker on the calendar in the next twelve months where a D-sel grade is in scope on either the unload or the load:

1. **Confirm the chemistry of the catalyst coming out and the catalyst going in.** NiW out and NiW in is one job. NiMo out and NiW in is a different briefing for the dump crew and a different sulphiding plan for operations. The day-of-job pack should state both.

2. **Pull the Topsoe loading procedure document number into the day-of-job pack.** A trade name is not enough. The procedure number, the revision date, the bed-by-bed scheme, and the dense-loading vibration spec all live in a Topsoe-supplied document that the loading contractor should have on the floor before the head opens.

3. **Adjust the inert-entry duration assumption upward.** Skinned beds take longer to dump than clean beds. A schedule built off the previous cycle's dump time on a different catalyst is not a schedule for this one.

4. **Stage the BA cylinder swap plan for the worst-case dump duration, not the expected one.** Pressure on the BA bottles is what creates the short-cuts. The plan should be conservative.

5. **Watch the air-purge passivation TC trace personally.** The panel reads what the panel reads. The crew lead's eyes on the local skin temperatures during the air introduction are what catch a sharper exotherm than the previous-cycle baseline predicted.

For young technicians evaluating a future in hydrocracker catalyst-changeout work, the broader reading: the same forces that determine the catalyst inside the reactor — diesel margin, crack spread, winter-grade premium, supplier roadmap — determine the work the crew sees at the next turnaround. The bed never picks itself. Someone in a procurement meeting six months before the changeout picked it. The crew lead's job is to know which case study they are working inside.
