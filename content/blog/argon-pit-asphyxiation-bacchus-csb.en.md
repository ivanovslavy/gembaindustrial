There's a pit beneath an autoclave at a propulsion-systems plant in Magna, Utah. (An autoclave is a big sealed oven that bakes parts under heat and pressure.) The crews who work there just call it "the pit." On the morning of January 30, 2023, two employees walked into it, lost consciousness, and never walked out.

The killer was argon. Argon is an inert gas — it doesn't react with anything and it can't burn — and the plant uses it to control the air inside the autoclave while composite parts cure. That argon had drifted down and collected in the pit.

The CSB — the U.S. Chemical Safety Board, the federal agency that investigates chemical accidents — covered this case in a few paragraphs of its *Volume 4 of Chemical Incident Reports*, released on February 18, 2026. The lesson it draws is one sentence: *the need for better protections for workers handling inert gases in confined spaces.* That sentence is correct. But it's where the conversation starts, not where it ends. This piece is the longer version, written for the people who read incident reports as part of their week — turnaround managers, HSE leads, breathing-apparatus specialists at the manway, young technicians on their first inert-entry permit.

## What the closure form said and what the report says

Northrop Grumman Bacchus Propulsion Systems makes the casings for solid-fuel rocket motors out of composite materials. At the heart of that work is the autoclave — a large pressure vessel that bakes layered composite under high heat and pressure in a controlled gas atmosphere. Below the autoclave sits a service pit: drainage, plumbing, instrument runs. In every way that matters, it's a confined space. Whether it counted as one *on paper* depended on how the local permit programme classified it.

The CSB's summary in *Volume 4* is short and clinical: argon was released into an enclosed area where two employees were working, both were asphyxiated, and the case "highlights risks associated with inert gases in confined spaces." The agency doesn't name the workers in its press release. Local news in Utah does, but for a contractor blog it's right to leave them anonymous — the lesson doesn't depend on who they were. We'll call them the first entrant and the second worker.

Here are the bare facts from the public record:

- The entrant went down into the pit during a normal shift in late January 2023.
- Argon, the inert gas used in the autoclave's curing atmosphere, had built up in the pit — enough to drive out the oxygen.
- A second worker went in to help, or to work alongside.
- Both were found unresponsive. Both died.
- The Utah Occupational Safety and Health Administration (UOSH) first issued *willful, serious* citations to the operator. Those were later downgraded to *serious* in a settlement, which closed off the path to a criminal referral and kept the operator off OSHA's Severe Violator Enforcement Program list.

Stop the story at the closure form and you get a tidy phrase: "asphyxiation in confined space." Read it as a contractor planning the next inert-gas job, and you get a much longer list of questions.

## Why argon, of all gases

Argon is about 1.4 times heavier than air. You can't smell it. You can't taste it. It isn't poisonous the way a textbook means it — your body has no reaction to it at all. Argon kills by absence. It pushes the oxygen out of the space you're standing in.

That weight — 1.4 times denser than air — is what makes this case so dangerous. A heavy inert gas doesn't behave like a leak of sour gas from a cracked flange that makes you gag and step back. Argon flows downhill. It pools. It fills sumps and pits and drains and just sits there. Walk down a stair into a pit and you pass through a layer of breathable air at the top, then your head crosses an invisible line, and suddenly the air around your face has almost no oxygen in it.

HSE's *L101 — Safe work in confined spaces* (the official UK guidance under the Confined Spaces Regulations 1997) is blunt about what that does to a body:

> *Inhaling an atmosphere containing no oxygen causes loss of consciousness in a matter of seconds because such an atmosphere not only fails to provide fresh oxygen, but also removes that already present in the bloodstream. At the same time, carbon dioxide is removed from the blood disrupting the normal 'breathe' reflex.*

Read that twice. *Loss of consciousness in a matter of seconds.* *Removes oxygen already present in the bloodstream.* The "breathe" reflex — the alarm your brain fires when carbon dioxide builds up — never goes off, because the inert gas is washing the carbon dioxide out of you with every breath. There's no warning. No urge to leave. Your body doesn't know it's dying.

The training card has a paragraph on this. The paragraph isn't wrong. But a paragraph is nothing like watching a colleague's knees buckle while you feel completely fine. The training doesn't prepare you for that.

## The 60% number

The CSB has been here before. In June 2003 it issued a *Safety Bulletin on the Hazards of Nitrogen Asphyxiation*. (The bulletin is about nitrogen, but the physics is the same for argon — both are inert, and both push out oxygen.) It reviewed 85 incidents in the United States between roughly 1992 and 2002: eighty deaths, fifty injuries. The CSB's then-Chairman, Carolyn W. Merritt, summed it up in a line worth keeping in your back pocket:

> *Nitrogen does not support life, and when nitrogen displaces the oxygen we breathe, it can prove very deadly.*

Tucked in the bulletin's appendix is the number that matters most for this blog's readers: **over 60% of the deaths in the dataset were contractors.** Not staff operators. Contractors.

That number is twenty years old now, and the share hasn't really improved since. The reason is built into how the work is organised. Contractors are the ones sent into these spaces during turnarounds, during catalyst changeouts, during the shutdowns when the inert gas is in play — and exactly when the gas-detector calibration logs are being handed back and forth between owner and contractor. The hand-off between the two organisations — operations releasing the asset, the contractor accepting it — is the very seam where testing assumptions can quietly drift apart.

The pattern is so reliable you can almost read it off the org chart. The host plant knows its piping. The contractor knows its tools. Nobody owns the question *is the inert gas physically cut off from the air my crew is going to breathe?* unless someone forces it onto the permit.

## The rescue impulse

There's a second pattern in the CSB's nitrogen bulletin, and it shows up in the Bacchus pit too: the second-victim effect. One worker drops. A colleague sees it and goes in to help. Both die.

The 2003 bulletin documents this again and again across its case studies, including a Valero refinery case (the 2005 nitrogen asphyxiation at Valero Delaware) where a contractor entrant was killed and a would-be rescuer was killed right beside him. The logic is always the same. A person who has collapsed in an oxygen-starved space is invisible to the rescuer — the air in the room looks exactly like the air in the corridor — and the rescuer's training says speed saves lives, which it usually does. So they cross the line. Loss of consciousness in a matter of seconds. Two bodies on the deck instead of one.

You can't train this impulse out of a person. You can only engineer around it. The procedural answer is a retrieval system: a tripod or davit over the manway, a full-body harness on the entrant, a winch line clipped on before they go in, a standby attendant who *never* enters under any circumstance, full stop, and a separate rescue team trained for inert-atmosphere recovery using breathing gear that works where there's zero oxygen — an SCBA (self-contained breathing apparatus, carrying its own air), not an air-purifying respirator. An air purifier filters bad stuff out of the air; it can't conjure oxygen out of nothing.

If your permit-to-work for an inert-gas job doesn't name the rescue team — by name, by phone number, by SCBA serial number, with confirmation they're on-site and standing by — the permit is incomplete. Not "a bit less rigorous than ideal." Incomplete. The CSB's nitrogen bulletin reads like a stack of footnotes about what happens when the rescue plan was a phone number to dial.

## What the training card does not cover

Here's what the eight-hour confined-space class covers well: the legal definitions, the four-gas monitor, the buddy system, the permit. Here's what it covers poorly — and what the Bacchus pit drags back into the light.

**Heavy gas pooling at the lowest point.** Testing the air at the lip of the manway is necessary, but it isn't enough. HSE's L101 is explicit: *the test should encompass the entire volume, especially the deepest areas where a dense gas may be present.* Argon settles at the bottom of the pit. If your four-gas tester reads 20.9% oxygen at the manway and you step in without first dropping the probe down to the deepest point, you tested the wrong air. The probe extension is a basic accessory. It's also the thing that's missing from the bag half the time. Check the bag.

**Continuous monitoring while someone's inside.** A pre-entry test is a single snapshot. A leak that develops *during* the work — a relief valve that lifts, a valve someone bumps open in the next unit over — never shows up in a snapshot. A continuous oxygen monitor, set to alarm at 19.5%, wired to the standby's wrist as well as the entrant's belt, is the difference between *we tested* and *we know.*

**Locking out the argon supply.** A confined-space permit and a hot-work permit are familiar. An *inert-gas isolation permit* is less so, but it carries just as much weight. If the autoclave's argon supply line runs anywhere near where the pit vents, that line needs positive isolation — a slip blind, or a double-block-and-bleed with the bleed checked open — for the whole entry. A closed valve isn't isolation. A locked closed valve is closer. A line that's been blanked off and bled to nothing is isolation.

**Locking out the equipment next door.** This is the one that catches people. The pit sits below the autoclave. The autoclave runs cycles. Those cycles purge with argon. If the entry permit isn't tied to the autoclave's purge schedule — if operations can run a normal cycle on the gear above the people in the pit — then your whole air-quality assumption rests on the goodwill of one coordinator with a clipboard. Goodwill fails on the third Tuesday of the month at shift handover.

**The standby's job description.** The standby attendant has exactly one job: keep eyes on the entrant and call for rescue. Not also mind the radio for the unit operator. Not also walk down the line to grab a torque wrench. Not also sign for a delivery. *Only* the standby job. If your standby is wearing a second hat, you don't have a standby — you have a coincidence.

## The Utah downgrade and what it means for risk-modelling

What happened to this incident *after* the accident matters too. UOSH first hit the operator with *willful, serious* citations. *Willful* means the agency believed the violation was knowing — that the employer was on notice the hazard existed and went ahead anyway. *Willful, serious* citations are unusual. In Utah they trigger a referral to the local district attorney to review for criminal prosecution, and they put the employer on the federal Severe Violator Enforcement Program (SVEP) — a published list that operators read carefully when deciding who to do business with.

Per the public reporting in Utah, those citations were later downgraded to *serious* in a settlement. The criminal-referral path closed. The SVEP listing never happened.

For a contractor deciding where to send a crew, the regulatory record is a primary input to the risk model. SVEP listings, willful citations, repeat-offender flags — these are the signals a pre-job hazard assessment leans on when the contractor's office is weighing whether to bid a job, what hazard premium to price in, what extra controls to demand at contract negotiation. When the public record ends up softer than the underlying facts deserved, the next contractor through that pit is making decisions on incomplete data. Fortunately the CSB report isn't affected by the state-level downgrade. Read both. Reconcile them yourself.

This isn't a political point. It's a procurement point. Your bid team needs to know.

## What a crew lead can do on the next inert-gas entry

The CSB's job is to draw conclusions for the whole industry. The crew lead's job is to make the next entry safer than the last. Here's the punch list this report adds to the standard permit:

1. **Walk the deepest point of the space with the four-gas monitor on a probe extension before anyone enters.** Write the lowest reading on the permit. If it's below 20.9% O₂, don't enter — find out where the displacement is coming from before you ventilate. Ventilation on its own, without controlling the source, gives you a clean reading that lasts exactly as long as the fan runs.

2. **Confirm positive isolation of every inert-gas source physically connected to the space.** Walk the line. Photograph the slip blind. Photograph the bleed valve open. Staple the photos to the permit packet. "Operations confirmed isolation" is not isolation; a slip blind you can see with your own eyes is isolation.

3. **Tie the entry to any nearby equipment that uses the same inert gas.** If the autoclave above the pit can run a cycle while the pit is occupied, your permit isn't isolated — it's merely coordinated, and coordination fails. Ask operations to formally put the autoclave on a "no purge" status for the entry window, with a tag on the local control panel.

4. **Continuous O₂ monitor at the deepest point, with two alarm levels: a remote audible at 19.5%, and a hard-wire to the standby's vibrating alarm at 19.0%.** The vibrating alarm matters because in a noisy plant, audible alarms get missed. A buzzer on your wrist doesn't.

5. **Name the rescue team on the permit.** Not "Plant Emergency." Actual names. SCBA confirmed donned at the manway. A drill that same morning, with a stopwatch, where the rescue team simulates "person down at the deepest point" — not "person down at the manway" — and times themselves. If the time-to-recover is longer than the entrant's airline-cylinder reserve, you've got a paper plan, not a real one.

6. **Bar the standby from any second duty.** Print it on the permit if you have to. The standby attends. The standby does not radio, does not walk the line, does not sign for deliveries. If you need a second person for those jobs, send a second person.

None of these six items is new. All six show up in the CSB's reports, in HSE's L101 ACOP, in the OSHA 1910.146 permit-required confined-spaces standard, and in IOGP's *Inert atmosphere protection* guidance. The new part isn't the list. The new part is pulling all six onto the *same permit* for an inert-gas job everyone has done a hundred times before. Familiarity is the single most common pre-condition to a Bacchus-shaped event. The job that's been done a hundred times is the job that gets the simplified permit.

## The thing the report does not put in writing

Inert-gas asphyxiation is the quietest way an industrial worker can die. No fire, no flash, no blood, no scream — the body just stops responding. Crews who've run airline-supplied entries for years describe the second-victim impulse the same way: you'd do it too. You'd step in to grab your colleague's arm. You wouldn't be able to stop yourself. The procedure is the only thing standing between you and that impulse.

The CSB's *Volume 4* lesson — "the need for better protections for workers handling inert gases in confined spaces" — is the official translation of that. The contractor's translation is shorter. Test the deepest part of the space. Positively isolate the inert-gas source. Name the rescue team on paper. And let the standby's only job be the standby's only job.

One pit, two workers, a few seconds. The report is short. The procedural change runs longer. Walk the deepest point of your next inert-entry space with the probe before you sign the permit. That's what the closure form doesn't capture, and what this report should be remembered for.
