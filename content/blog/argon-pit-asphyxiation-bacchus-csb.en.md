There is a pit beneath an autoclave at a propulsion-systems plant in Magna, Utah. The crews who work there call it "the pit." On the morning of January 30, 2023, two employees walked into it, lost consciousness, and never walked out. Argon — the inert gas used to manage the curing atmosphere of the composite parts the autoclave processes — had collected in the pit. The CSB's *Volume 4 of Chemical Incident Reports*, released on February 18, 2026, devotes a few paragraphs to the case. The lesson the report draws is one sentence: *the need for better protections for workers handling inert gases in confined spaces.* That sentence is correct. It is also the start of the conversation, not the end of it. This piece is the longer version, written for the people who read incident reports as part of their week — turnaround managers, HSE leads, BA specialists at the manway, young technicians on their first inert-entry permit.

## What the closure form said and what the report says

Northrop Grumman Bacchus Propulsion Systems makes solid-fuel rocket motor cases out of composite materials. The autoclave at the heart of the process is a large pressure vessel that subjects layered composite to high heat and pressure under a controlled gas atmosphere. Below the autoclave sits a service pit — drainage, plumbing, instrument runs. It is a confined space in everything but the legal sense, depending on how the local permit programme classified it.

The CSB's summary in *Volume 4* is short and clinical: argon was released into an enclosed area where two employees were working, both were asphyxiated, and the case "highlights risks associated with inert gases in confined spaces." The agency does not name the workers in the press release. Public reporting in Utah does, but anonymisation is the right convention for a contractor blog — the lesson does not depend on who. We will refer to them as the first entrant and the second worker.

The bare facts that follow from the public record:

- The entrant descended into the pit during a normal shift in late January 2023.
- Argon, the inert gas used in the autoclave's curing atmosphere, had accumulated in the pit at concentrations sufficient to cause oxygen depletion.
- A second worker entered to assist or to work alongside.
- Both were found unresponsive. Both died.
- The Utah Occupational Safety and Health Administration (UOSH) initially issued *willful, serious* citations to the operator. Those citations were later downgraded to *serious* in a settlement, which removed the referral path to a district attorney and the operator's potential addition to OSHA's Severe Violator Enforcement Program.

If you stop the story at the closure form, you get a tidy phrase: "asphyxiation in confined space." If you read it as a contractor planning the next inert-gas job, you get a much longer list of questions.

## Why argon, of all gases

Argon is roughly 1.4 times denser than air. It does not smell. It does not taste. It is not toxic in the textbook sense — your body does not react to it. Argon kills by absence: it pushes oxygen out of the volume you are standing in.

That density number — 1.4 — does the work in this case. A heavier-than-air inert gas does not behave like a leak from a cracked sour-gas flange that makes you gag and back up. Argon flows downhill. It pools. It fills sumps and pits and drain points and stays there. If you walk down a stair into a pit, you walk through a layer of breathable air at the top, then your head crosses an interface, and the air around your face is now oxygen-depleted.

HSE's *L101 — Safe work in confined spaces* (the Approved Code of Practice under the UK's Confined Spaces Regulations 1997) is direct about what this looks like physiologically:

> *Inhaling an atmosphere containing no oxygen causes loss of consciousness in a matter of seconds because such an atmosphere not only fails to provide fresh oxygen, but also removes that already present in the bloodstream. At the same time, carbon dioxide is removed from the blood disrupting the normal 'breathe' reflex.*

Read that twice. *Loss of consciousness in a matter of seconds.* *Removes oxygen already present in the bloodstream.* The "breathe" reflex — the thing your medulla oblongata does when CO₂ builds up — does not fire because CO₂ is being washed out of you with every breath of inert gas. There is no warning. There is no urge to leave. The body does not know it is dying.

The training card has a paragraph on this. The paragraph is not wrong. The paragraph is also nothing like watching a colleague's knees go and feeling no warning yourself. The training does not cover that.

## The 60% number

The CSB has been here before. In June 2003 the Board issued a *Safety Bulletin on the Hazards of Nitrogen Asphyxiation* (the bulletin is about nitrogen, but the physics applies to argon too — both are inert and both displace oxygen). The bulletin reviewed 85 incidents in the United States between roughly 1992 and 2002. Eighty deaths. Fifty injuries. The Bulletin's then-Chairman, Carolyn W. Merritt, summed it up in one line worth keeping in your back pocket:

> *Nitrogen does not support life, and when nitrogen displaces the oxygen we breathe, it can prove very deadly.*

Buried in the Bulletin's appendix is the number that matters most for this blog's audience: **over 60% of the fatalities in the dataset were contractors.** Not staff operators. Contractors.

That number is twenty years old now and the proportion has not improved much in the intervening decades. The reason is structural. Contractors are the people sent into the spaces during turnarounds, during catalyst changeouts, during the off-runs and shutdowns when the inert gas is in play and when the gas-detector calibration logs are in transition between owner and contractor. The hand-off between the two organisations — operations releasing the asset, the contractor accepting it — is exactly where atmospheric testing assumptions can quietly diverge.

The pattern is so consistent that you can almost predict it from the org chart. The host plant knows its piping. The contractor knows its tools. Neither owns the question *is the inert gas physically isolated from the volume my crew will breathe?* unless someone explicitly forces it onto the permit.

## The rescue impulse

There is a second pattern in the CSB's nitrogen bulletin and it shows up in the Bacchus pit too: the second-victim phenomenon. One worker collapses. A colleague sees them go down and goes in to help. Both die.

The 2003 Bulletin documents this repeatedly across its case studies, including a Valero refinery case study (the 2005 nitrogen asphyxiation at Valero Delaware) where a contractor entrant was killed and a would-be rescuer was killed alongside him. The structural logic is the same: a person who collapses inside an oxygen-deficient space is invisible to the rescuer's eyes — the air in the room looks identical to the air in the corridor — and the rescuer's training tells them speed matters, which it does. So they cross the threshold. Loss of consciousness in a matter of seconds. Two bodies on the deck instead of one.

You cannot train this impulse out of a human. You can only engineer around it. The procedural counterpart is a retrieval system — a tripod or davit at the manway, a full-body harness on the entrant, a winch line clipped before entry, a standby attendant who does not enter under any circumstance, period, and a separate rescue team trained for inert-atmosphere recovery with respiratory protection that can survive zero oxygen (an SCBA, not an air-purifying respirator — air purifiers cannot purify oxygen out of nothing).

If your permit-to-work for an inert-gas job does not name the rescue team, by name, by phone number, by SCBA serial number, with confirmation that they are on-site and standing by — the permit is incomplete. Not "less rigorous than ideal." Incomplete. The CSB's nitrogen bulletin reads as a series of footnotes documenting what happens when the rescue plan was a phone number to dial.

## What the training card does not cover

Here is what the eight-hour confined-space class does cover well: the regulatory definitions, the four-gas monitor, the buddy system, the permit. Here is what it does not cover well — and what the Bacchus pit forces back into focus.

**Dense-gas pooling at the deepest point.** Atmospheric testing at the manway lip is necessary and not sufficient. HSE's L101 is explicit on this: *the test should encompass the entire volume, especially the deepest areas where a dense gas may be present.* Argon pools at the bottom of the pit. If the four-gas tester reads 20.9% oxygen at the manway and you do not extend the probe down to the deepest point of the pit before stepping in, you are testing the wrong air. The probe extension is a basic accessory. It is also frequently missing from the bag. Check the bag.

**Continuous monitoring while occupied.** A pre-entry test is a snapshot. An inert gas leak that develops during the work — a relief that lifts, a valve that someone bumps open in an adjacent unit — is invisible to a snapshot. A continuous oxygen monitor, alarm-set at 19.5%, with the alarm wired to the standby's wrist as well as the entrant's belt, is the difference between *we tested* and *we know.*

**Argon supply lockout/tagout.** A confined-space permit and a hot-work permit are familiar. An *inert-gas isolation permit* is less so but exactly as load-bearing. If the autoclave's argon supply line passes anywhere within the venting envelope of the pit, that line needs a positive isolation — a slip blind, a double-block-and-bleed with the bleed verified open — for the duration of the entry. A closed valve is not isolation. A locked closed valve is closer. A blanked-and-bled line is isolation.

**Adjacent operations interlock.** This is the one that bites. The pit is below the autoclave. The autoclave runs cycles. The cycles purge with argon. If the entry permit does not interlock with the autoclave's purge schedule — if operations can run a normal cycle on the equipment above the people in the pit — your atmospheric assumption is built on the goodwill of a coordinator with a clipboard. Goodwill fails on the third Tuesday of the month at handover.

**The standby's job description.** The standby attendant's only job is to keep eyes on the entrant and to summon rescue. Not to also tend the radio for the unit operator. Not to also walk the line to grab a torque wrench. Not to also help receive a delivery. *Only* the standby job. If the standby has a second hat, you do not have a standby; you have a coincidence.

## The Utah downgrade and what it means for risk-modelling

The regulatory afterlife of this incident matters too. UOSH initially levelled *willful, serious* citations against the operator. *Willful* means the agency believed the violation was knowing — that the employer was on notice that a hazard existed and proceeded anyway. *Willful, serious* citations are unusual; in Utah they trigger a referral to the local district attorney for criminal prosecution review, and they enrol the employer in the federal Severe Violator Enforcement Program (SVEP), which is a published list operators read carefully when scoping who to do business with.

Per the public reporting in Utah, those citations were later downgraded to *serious* in a settlement. The criminal-referral path closed. The SVEP listing did not happen.

For a contractor evaluating where to send a crew, the regulatory record is a primary input to the risk model. SVEP listings, willful citations, repeat-offender flags — these are the signals that pre-job hazard assessments lean on when staff at the contractor's office are weighing whether to bid a job, what hazard premium to price in, what extra controls to insist on at contract negotiation. When the public regulatory record is softer than the underlying facts merited, the next contractor that comes through to do work at that pit is making decisions on incomplete data. The CSB report, fortunately, is not affected by the state-level downgrade. Read both. Reconcile them yourself.

This is not a political point. It is a procurement point. Your bid team needs to know.

## What a crew lead can do on the next inert-gas entry

The CSB's role is to draw conclusions for industry. The crew lead's role is to make the next entry safer than the last. Here is the punch list this report adds to the standard permit:

1. **Walk the deepest point of the space with the four-gas monitor on a probe extension before any entry.** Mark the lowest reading on the permit. If it is below 20.9% O₂, do not enter — investigate where the displacement is coming from before you ventilate. Ventilation alone, without source control, gives you a clean reading that lasts as long as the fan runs.

2. **Confirm positive isolation of every inert-gas source physically connected to the space.** Walk the line. Photograph the slip blind. Photograph the bleed valve open. Attach the photographs to the permit packet. "Operations confirmed isolation" is not isolation; a slip blind you can see is isolation.

3. **Interlock the entry with adjacent equipment that uses the same inert gas.** If the autoclave above the pit can run a cycle while the pit is occupied, your permit is not isolated, it is coordinated — and coordination fails. Ask operations to formally place the autoclave on a "no purge" status for the entry window, with a tag on the local control panel.

4. **Continuous O₂ monitor at the deepest point, with two alarm escalations: a remote audible at 19.5% and a hard-wire to the standby's vibrating alarm at 19.0%.** The vibrating-alarm part matters because in a noisy plant, audible alarms get missed. A device on the wrist does not.

5. **Name the rescue team on the permit.** Not "Plant Emergency." Names. SCBA confirmed donned at the manway. A drill that morning, with a stopwatch, where the rescue team simulates "person down at the deepest point" — not "person down at the manway" — and times themselves. If the time-to-recover exceeds the entrant's airline-cylinder reserve, you have a paper plan and not an actual one.

6. **Disqualify the standby from any second duty.** Print this on the permit if you have to. The standby attends. The standby does not radio, does not walk the line, does not sign for deliveries. If a second person is needed for those tasks, send a second person.

None of these six items is novel. All six show up in the CSB's reports, in HSE's L101 ACOP, in the OSHA 1910.146 permit-required confined-spaces standard, and in IOGP's *Inert atmosphere protection* guidance. The novelty is not in the list. The novelty is in pulling them all onto the *same permit* for an inert-gas job that everyone has done before. Familiarity is the most common pre-condition to a Bacchus-shaped event. The job that has been done a hundred times is the job that gets the simplified permit.

## The thing the report does not put in writing

Inert-gas asphyxiation is the cleanest way an industrial worker can die. There is no fire, no flash, no gore, no scream — the body simply stops responding. Crews who have run airline-supplied entries for years describe the second-victim impulse the same way: you would do it too. You would step in to grab your colleague's arm. You would not be able to help yourself. The procedure is the only thing standing between you and that impulse.

The CSB's *Volume 4* lesson — "the need for better protections for workers handling inert gases in confined spaces" — is the regulatory translation of that. The contractor's translation is shorter. Test the deepest part of the space. Isolate the inert-gas source positively. Name the rescue team on paper. And let the standby's only job be the standby's only job.

One pit, two workers, a few seconds. The report is short. The procedural change runs longer. Walk the deepest point of your next inert-entry space with the probe before you sign the permit. That is what the closure form does not capture and what this report should be remembered for.
